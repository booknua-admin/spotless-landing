If you have been running a residential cleaning business and wondering how to break into commercial work, or if you already service a few offices and want to scale, commercial contracts represent the clearest path to predictable, scalable revenue. A single commercial contract can equal the revenue of twenty to fifty residential clients, with the added benefit of recurring monthly payments and less client-by-client management.

But [commercial cleaning](/commercial) is a different game than residential. The bidding process is more formal, pricing mistakes are more costly, and client expectations are structured around contracts rather than relationships. This guide covers everything you need to know to bid, win, and profitably manage commercial cleaning contracts.

## Why Commercial Contracts Are the Path to Predictable Revenue

Residential cleaning revenue is inherently volatile. Clients skip weeks, cancel for vacations, move away, and churn at rates of 20 to 30 percent annually. Your revenue fluctuates month to month, making it hard to plan hiring, equipment purchases, or expansion.

Commercial contracts solve these problems in several ways:

- **Predictable income.** A twelve-month contract with a 10,000-square-foot office pays the same amount every month regardless of holidays or weather.
- **Higher job values.** A single commercial job might bill five hundred to several thousand dollars per visit. Achieving the same revenue in residential requires managing dozens of individual client relationships.
- **Lower marketing costs per dollar of revenue.** Landing one commercial contract replaces the marketing effort needed to acquire fifty residential clients.
- **Longer client lifecycles.** Commercial clients who are happy with your service renew year after year. Many commercial cleaning relationships last five to ten years or more.
- **Daytime or after-hours flexibility.** Most commercial cleaning happens in the evening or early morning, which means you can run commercial and residential operations simultaneously without schedule conflicts.

The trade-off is that commercial work requires more upfront investment in the bidding process, higher insurance requirements, and the ability to staff larger teams for bigger facilities. But for cleaning businesses serious about growth, commercial contracts are where the leverage is.

## Understanding the Commercial Bidding Process

Commercial clients select cleaning vendors through a more structured process than residential clients. Understanding how this works gives you a significant advantage over competitors who treat commercial bids like oversized residential quotes.

### Requests for Proposals (RFPs)

Larger facilities and property management companies issue formal RFPs that outline their cleaning requirements, schedule expectations, and evaluation criteria. An RFP typically includes the facility specifications, required services, frequency expectations, insurance requirements, and submission deadlines.

Find RFPs through commercial real estate networks, property management company websites, government procurement portals for public buildings, and industry contacts. Building relationships with facility managers and property managers before RFPs are issued gives you advance notice and insight into what they prioritize.

### The Walkthrough

Never bid on a commercial job without completing an in-person walkthrough. This is where you gather the information that makes your bid accurate and competitive.

During the walkthrough, document everything:

- **Total square footage** broken down by area type: office space, restrooms, kitchen or breakroom, lobbies, hallways, conference rooms, and specialty areas like server rooms or labs.
- **Floor types** in each area: carpet, VCT, hardwood, tile, concrete, or specialty flooring. Different surfaces require different equipment, chemicals, and time.
- **Restroom count and fixture count.** Restrooms drive a disproportionate amount of labor time. Count toilets, urinals, sinks, and stalls.
- **Current condition.** Is the facility well-maintained and needs routine upkeep, or has it been neglected and needs a recovery clean before regular service begins?
- **Occupancy patterns.** How many people use the space daily? High-traffic areas need more frequent attention.
- **Special requirements.** Security protocols, alarm codes, restricted areas, green cleaning mandates, or specific chemical restrictions.
- **Existing pain points.** Ask the facility manager what problems they have had with previous cleaning providers. This tells you exactly what to emphasize in your proposal.

Take photos during the walkthrough with permission. You will reference them when building your labor estimates.

## How to Price Commercial Contracts

Pricing commercial contracts correctly is the difference between profitability and working at a loss for twelve months. There are three common pricing models, and each has appropriate applications.

### Per Square Foot Pricing

This is the most common model for office buildings and general commercial spaces. You calculate a per-square-foot rate based on the service frequency, facility type, and your local market.

Typical per-square-foot rates for standard janitorial service range from three to twelve cents per square foot per visit, depending on your market, the facility type, and the scope of work. A 20,000-square-foot office cleaned five nights per week at six cents per square foot generates six thousand dollars per month.

To calculate your rate, work backwards from your costs:

1. Estimate total labor hours per visit based on your walkthrough data. Use production rates, the square footage one cleaner can handle per hour, for each area type. A general office might be 3,500 to 5,000 square feet per hour. Restrooms might be 500 to 800 square feet per hour.
2. Multiply labor hours by your fully loaded labor cost, which includes wages, payroll taxes, workers' comp, and benefits.
3. Add supply costs, typically three to five percent of the contract value.
4. Add equipment depreciation and maintenance costs.
5. Add overhead allocation: insurance, vehicle costs, administrative time, software, and management.
6. Add your profit margin, typically ten to twenty percent for established companies.
7. Divide the total by square footage to get your per-square-foot rate.

### Per Hour Pricing

Hourly pricing works well for smaller facilities or when the scope of work varies significantly. It is transparent and easy for clients to understand, but it offers less profit upside than per-square-foot pricing because you are essentially selling labor rather than a service outcome.

Hourly rates for commercial cleaning typically range from twenty-five to fifty dollars per labor hour billed to the client, depending on your market and the type of work.

### Per Service Pricing

Some contracts price specific services individually: floor stripping and waxing, carpet cleaning, window washing, and pressure washing are commonly priced per service rather than bundled into a flat rate. This approach works well for periodic services that supplement a base janitorial contract.

## What to Include in Your Commercial Proposal

Your proposal is a sales document, not just a price sheet. It needs to communicate your professionalism, reliability, and understanding of the client's specific needs.

### Essential Proposal Sections

**Executive Summary.** Two to three paragraphs summarizing your understanding of the client's needs and how your company is positioned to meet them. Reference specific details from the walkthrough to show you were paying attention.

**Scope of Work.** A detailed breakdown of every task you will perform, organized by area and frequency. Be specific. Do not write "clean restrooms." Write "sanitize all toilet bowls, urinals, and seats. Clean and polish sinks and fixtures. Refill soap, paper towel, and toilet paper dispensers. Mop floors with disinfectant. Empty trash and replace liners. Clean mirrors. Wipe partitions and door handles."

**Frequency Schedule.** Clearly state which tasks happen daily, weekly, monthly, quarterly, and annually. Use a matrix format for easy scanning.

**Staffing Plan.** How many cleaners will be assigned, what hours they will work, and who supervises them. Name a dedicated account manager or site supervisor.

**Quality Assurance Program.** Describe your inspection process, reporting cadence, and how you handle issues. This section differentiates you from low-bid competitors who offer no accountability.

**Pricing.** Present your pricing clearly with the monthly total prominently displayed. If you offer tiered options such as basic, standard, and premium, present all three so the client has a choice rather than a take-it-or-leave-it number.

**Company Background.** Briefly cover your experience, insurance coverage, certifications, and relevant client references. Three to five references from similar facility types carry significant weight.

**Terms and Conditions.** Contract length, cancellation terms, price escalation clauses, and payment terms.

## Contract Terms: Scope, Frequency, Termination, and Price Escalation

The contract itself protects both you and the client. Do not operate on a handshake, even with small commercial accounts.

### Scope Definition

Define the scope precisely. The most common source of commercial cleaning disputes is scope creep, where clients expect tasks that were never included in the contract. If window cleaning is not in the scope, state that explicitly. If the contract covers common areas but not individual offices, document the boundaries.

### Frequency and Schedule

Specify exact days and times of service. If the contract calls for five-day service Monday through Friday, state whether holidays are included or excluded. Define what happens when a scheduled cleaning day falls on a holiday: is it skipped, rescheduled, or does the price adjust?

### Termination Clauses

Standard commercial cleaning contracts include a mutual termination clause requiring thirty to sixty days written notice. This protects you from losing a contract overnight while giving the client an exit path if they are dissatisfied. Some contracts include a cure period, typically fifteen to thirty days, where you have the opportunity to fix documented deficiencies before termination takes effect.

### Price Escalation

Build an annual price escalation clause into every contract. Labor costs, supply costs, and insurance premiums increase every year. A three to five percent annual increase is standard and expected by most facility managers. Without this clause, you lock yourself into pricing that erodes your margins year over year.

### Payment Terms

Net 30 is standard for commercial cleaning. Some clients push for net 45 or net 60. Be cautious with extended terms because cash flow is critical for cleaning businesses that need to make payroll weekly or biweekly. Track all invoicing and payment timelines through [financial management tools](/product/finance) to avoid cash flow surprises.

## Managing Multi-Site Contracts

Multi-site contracts with property management companies, retail chains, or corporate campuses represent significant revenue opportunities but add operational complexity.

### Consistent Quality Across Locations

The client expects the same standard at every site. This requires standardized SOPs, consistent training, and a quality inspection program that covers all locations. Assign a dedicated account manager for multi-site contracts who visits each location regularly and serves as the single point of contact for the client.

### Staffing and Scheduling Logistics

Multi-site contracts require careful [scheduling](/product/scheduling) to ensure each location is staffed appropriately. Consider geographic clustering to minimize travel time between sites. Use zone-based team assignments so the same cleaners service the same locations consistently, which improves quality through familiarity with each space.

### Centralized Reporting

Multi-site clients expect consolidated reporting. Provide monthly reports that cover all locations with individual site scorecards, issue logs, and resolution summaries. This level of reporting is labor-intensive without software, but it is what separates professional commercial cleaning operators from small operators who cannot scale.

## Quality Assurance and Reporting for Commercial Clients

Commercial clients evaluate you on outcomes, not effort. A structured quality assurance program demonstrates accountability and gives you data to defend your performance when issues arise.

### Inspection Programs

Conduct regular inspections using a standardized scoring system. Rate each area on a one-to-five scale across dimensions like cleanliness, restocking, floor condition, and trash removal. Track scores over time to identify trends. Share inspection reports with the client monthly.

### Client Communication Cadence

Establish a regular communication rhythm with your primary contact, usually the facility manager. Monthly check-in calls or meetings work well for most contracts. Quarterly business reviews for larger contracts give you an opportunity to discuss performance data, upcoming needs, and potential scope adjustments.

### Issue Resolution Protocols

Define a clear process for handling complaints. When a facility manager reports an issue, acknowledge it within two hours, investigate within 24 hours, and resolve it within 48 hours. Document every issue and its resolution. Over time, this log becomes evidence of your responsiveness and continuous improvement.

## Building Long-Term Relationships With Facility Managers

The facility manager is your most important relationship in commercial cleaning. They control vendor selection, contract renewals, and referrals to other properties in their portfolio.

### Understand Their Pressures

Facility managers are judged on building occupant satisfaction, cost control, and operational efficiency. Your job is to make them look good by delivering consistent quality and proactive communication. When you spot a maintenance issue during cleaning, like a leaking faucet or a burnt-out light, report it to the facility manager. This positions you as a partner, not just a vendor.

### Be Proactive, Not Reactive

Do not wait for complaints to communicate. Send proactive updates about seasonal preparations, equipment upgrades, or staffing changes that might affect service. If you are bringing in a new team member for their building, let them know in advance. If you are planning to strip and refinish the floors next month, confirm the schedule early.

### Add Value Beyond the Contract

Offer facility managers value that goes beyond cleaning: provide recommendations for pest control, landscaping, or other building services based on what your team observes. Share industry knowledge about new cleaning technologies or sustainable practices. The more value you provide, the harder it becomes for a competitor to displace you on price alone.

## Common Mistakes in Commercial Cleaning Bids

Learning from common mistakes saves you from painful lessons that erode profitability and damage your reputation.

### Underbidding to Win the Contract

The most destructive mistake is pricing below your costs to win a contract, assuming you will make up the margin through efficiency gains. You rarely do. Instead, you end up cutting corners on labor, which reduces quality, which triggers complaints, which leads to contract termination. Bid accurately, and if your price is too high for a particular client, walk away. Unprofitable contracts are worse than no contracts.

### Ignoring the Walkthrough Details

A lazy walkthrough produces an inaccurate bid. If you miss that the facility has twelve restrooms instead of eight, or that half the floors are VCT requiring periodic stripping, your labor estimates will be wrong and your margins will suffer for the duration of the contract.

### Vague Scope Definitions

If your proposal says "clean the building," you have set yourself up for every possible interpretation. When the client expects window washing and you thought it was only janitorial, the dispute damages the relationship regardless of who is technically right. Be exhaustively specific in your scope of work.

### No Quality Assurance Program

Bidding without a quality assurance section tells the facility manager that you have no system for maintaining standards. Even if your price is competitive, this gap signals risk. Most experienced facility managers have been burned by low-cost providers with no accountability. Show them you are different.

### Failing to Include Price Escalation

Locking into a three-year contract without annual price increases is a guaranteed margin squeeze. Labor costs rise every year. Supply costs rise every year. Your insurance premium rises every year. A contract without escalation becomes less profitable each year until you are essentially subsidizing the client's cleaning costs.

### Underestimating Supply Costs

Supply costs seem minor on individual jobs but compound across large contracts. Restroom supplies, trash liners, cleaning chemicals, and equipment consumables add up. Budget three to five percent of contract value for supplies, and track actual spend monthly to verify your estimates.

## Getting Started With Commercial Contracts

If you are transitioning from residential to commercial or expanding your commercial portfolio, start with these steps:

1. **Build your commercial credentials.** Ensure your insurance limits meet commercial requirements, typically one to two million dollars in general liability. Get bonded if you are not already.
2. **Start small.** Target small offices, medical practices, or retail spaces with 2,000 to 10,000 square feet. These are manageable to learn on and often do not require a formal RFP process.
3. **Perfect your walkthrough and estimating process.** Do five walkthroughs before you submit your first bid. Practice documenting every detail.
4. **Create your proposal template.** Build a professional, thorough template that you customize for each prospect.
5. **Network with facility managers and property managers.** Attend local commercial real estate events and building owner association meetings.
6. **Track everything.** Use [Spotless scheduling](/product/scheduling) and [financial tools](/product/finance) to manage commercial operations with the precision these clients expect.

Commercial cleaning is not harder than residential. It is different. The companies that approach it with the right systems, pricing discipline, and relationship-building skills find it to be the most rewarding segment of the cleaning industry.
