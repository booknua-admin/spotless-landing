# Commercial Cleaning Contracts: How to Bid, Win, and Manage

Commercial cleaning is where cleaning businesses go to build predictable, scalable revenue. A single commercial contract can be worth more than 20 residential clients combined, it pays on a set schedule, and it does not cancel because the homeowner decided to clean the house themselves this week. But commercial work also comes with higher stakes, longer sales cycles, and razor-thin margins if you price it wrong.

If you have been running a residential cleaning operation and you are looking at commercial contracts as your next growth move — or if you are starting fresh and want to go straight into commercial — this guide walks you through every step. From understanding the types of contracts available to conducting walkthroughs, building bids, writing scope of work documents, and managing accounts once you win them.

No theory. Just the mechanics of how commercial cleaning pricing and contract management actually work.

## Types of Commercial Cleaning Contracts

Not all commercial contracts are the same. The type of facility, the client's expectations, and the contract structure all vary significantly. Understanding these categories helps you target the right opportunities and price them correctly.

### Office Cleaning

This is the bread and butter of commercial cleaning. Standard office cleaning typically includes vacuuming, trash removal, restroom sanitation, kitchen or break room cleaning, dusting, and surface wiping. Offices are cleaned after business hours, usually five nights per week, though some smaller offices need service only two or three times per week.

Office cleaning is high-frequency, low-complexity work. The margins are thinner per visit, but the volume and consistency make it profitable. A 10,000-square-foot office cleaned five nights a week at $0.08 per square foot generates $3,200 per month in recurring revenue. Land five of those and you have $16,000 per month in predictable income before you even think about residential.

### Medical and Dental Facility Cleaning

Healthcare facilities require specialized cleaning protocols, including proper handling of biohazardous waste, use of hospital-grade disinfectants, and compliance with OSHA and HIPAA regulations. The bar is higher, and so is the pay. Medical cleaning contracts typically command 30% to 50% higher rates than standard office cleaning.

You need specific training and certifications for this niche — OSHA bloodborne pathogen training at minimum — but the investment pays off. Medical facilities are also less likely to switch providers because of the compliance burden involved in onboarding a new cleaning company. Once you are in, you tend to stay in.

### Retail and Restaurant Cleaning

Retail spaces need floor care (stripping, waxing, buffing), window cleaning, and restroom maintenance. Restaurants add kitchen deep cleaning, grease trap maintenance, and health code compliance to the mix. These contracts often include day porter services — a cleaner who is on-site during business hours handling messes as they happen.

Retail and restaurant contracts tend to be demanding and time-sensitive. Cleaning must be completed within a narrow window before opening or after closing. Price accordingly — the time constraint alone justifies a premium.

### Industrial and Warehouse Cleaning

Warehouses, manufacturing plants, and distribution centers need large-area floor scrubbing, high-dusting, pressure washing, and sometimes hazardous material handling. The equipment requirements are significant — you are talking ride-on scrubbers, industrial vacuums, and aerial lifts. This niche has high barriers to entry but also the highest per-contract revenue. A single warehouse contract can run $8,000 to $25,000 per month.

### Construction Cleanup

Post-construction cleaning (rough clean, final clean, and touch-up clean) is project-based rather than recurring. The work is intense — removing construction dust from every surface, cleaning windows with sticker residue, scrubbing grout, and polishing fixtures. It pays well per job but does not provide recurring revenue unless you build relationships with general contractors who feed you projects consistently.

## How to Find Commercial Cleaning Opportunities

Commercial contracts do not usually come from Google Ads or Yelp reviews. The sales process is different from residential, and you need to meet it where it lives.

### Direct Outreach

Cold outreach works in commercial cleaning because facility managers and office managers are actively dealing with cleaning problems. Their current vendor missed the restrooms again, or the floors look terrible, or they are paying too much for mediocre service. A well-timed email or phone call can open a conversation.

Build a target list of 50 to 100 facilities in your service area. Research the property management company or the office manager's name. Send a personalized email that references something specific about their building or business. Follow up by phone three to five days later. Most commercial cleaning contracts are won through persistent, professional outreach — not passive marketing.

Here is a cold email framework that gets responses:

Subject: Quick question about [Building Name] cleaning

Body: "Hi [Name], I noticed [specific observation about their building — could be from a visit, their Google reviews, or their website]. We specialize in [relevant service] for [their facility type] in [area]. I would like to offer a complimentary walkthrough and cleaning assessment — no commitment, just an honest evaluation of your current setup with recommendations. Would next Tuesday or Thursday work for a 20-minute visit?"

### Property Management Companies

Property management firms control cleaning contracts for multiple buildings. Landing a relationship with one property manager can give you access to five, ten, or more facilities. These relationships take time to build, but they are the highest-leverage commercial sales channel available.

Attend local commercial real estate events and join your regional BOMA (Building Owners and Managers Association) chapter. These are the rooms where property managers spend time and where they talk about which vendors are delivering and which are not.

### Bid Boards and RFPs

Larger facilities and government buildings post formal Requests for Proposals (RFPs) on procurement websites. GSA.gov handles federal contracts. State and municipal procurement sites handle local government buildings. Many private companies also post RFPs through platforms like BidNet or Dodge Construction Network.

RFPs are competitive and documentation-heavy, but they give you access to large, stable contracts. Government cleaning contracts in particular are prized because they pay reliably and often run for multiple years with renewal options.

### Networking and Referrals

Just like residential cleaning, referrals drive commercial business. Ask your current commercial clients if they know other facility managers who might need cleaning services. Offer a referral credit — $200 to $500 off their next invoice — for introductions that convert. Facility managers talk to each other, especially within the same industry or property management network.

## The Walkthrough Inspection: Where Bids Are Won or Lost

Never bid on a commercial contract without a walkthrough. Photos, floor plans, and square footage numbers from a listing do not tell you what you need to know. You have to walk the space in person.

### What to Evaluate During a Walkthrough

**Square footage and layout.** Measure or verify the total cleanable square footage. Note how the space is divided — open floor plans clean faster than spaces with many small offices, conference rooms, and closets. A 10,000-square-foot open-plan office cleans in roughly half the time of a 10,000-square-foot space divided into 40 individual offices.

**Flooring types.** Different floors require different equipment and cleaning methods. Carpet needs vacuuming and periodic hot water extraction. VCT (vinyl composition tile) needs stripping and waxing on a quarterly or annual cycle. Hardwood needs specific pH-neutral products to avoid damage. Polished concrete in warehouse spaces needs auto-scrubbing. Count the square footage of each flooring type separately because your labor estimate depends on it.

**Restroom count and condition.** Restrooms are the most time-intensive areas per square foot in any commercial facility. Count the number of restrooms, stalls, sinks, and urinals. Note the condition — heavily used restrooms with old grout and hard water stains require significantly more time than modern, well-maintained ones. A single high-traffic restroom with six stalls can take one cleaner 25 to 35 minutes to service properly.

**Window count and accessibility.** Interior glass (office partitions, conference room walls) and exterior windows need different approaches and equipment. Note how many, how high, and whether they are accessible from inside or require ladders or lifts.

**Kitchen and break room.** How many sinks, refrigerators, microwaves, and coffee machines? Does the client expect appliance interior cleaning, or just surface wiping and trash removal? Clarify this during the walkthrough — break room expectations are one of the most common sources of scope disagreements.

**Special requirements.** Server rooms that need restricted access. Areas with sensitive equipment that cannot get wet. Security protocols. Alarm codes. Loading dock access for bringing in floor equipment. All of this affects your operational costs and must be factored into the bid.

**Current cleaning issues.** Ask the facility manager what they are unhappy about with their current service. This is gold. It tells you what they value most and exactly where you can differentiate your proposal. If they complain about restroom quality, you know to emphasize your restroom protocols. If they are frustrated by poor communication, lead with your reporting and check-in cadence.

### Questions to Ask the Facility Manager

- What cleaning schedule are you looking for? (Nightly, three times per week, day porter?)
- Are there areas of the building that are off-limits or require escort?
- How many employees use the facility daily? What about visitors?
- Do you supply consumables (paper towels, toilet paper, soap) or do you want us to include them?
- What is your budget range for cleaning services? (Many will not answer, but it is worth asking.)
- When does the current contract expire?
- What is your decision timeline and who makes the final decision?
- Have you had issues with previous cleaning companies? What specifically went wrong?

Document everything with photos and detailed notes. You will need this information to build an accurate bid, and the thoroughness of your walkthrough signals professionalism to the prospect.

## How to Price Commercial Cleaning Contracts

Pricing commercial work is more structured than residential pricing. You are building a bid that will be compared directly against other companies, so your numbers need to be both competitive and profitable. Underbid and you bleed money for the life of the contract. Overbid and you lose the deal.

### The Square Footage Method

Most commercial cleaning is priced per square foot per visit or per month. Here are the 2026 benchmark ranges for standard janitorial service:

- **Standard office cleaning** (nightly, 5x/week): $0.05 to $0.15 per square foot per month
- **Medical facility cleaning**: $0.10 to $0.25 per square foot per month
- **Retail space cleaning**: $0.06 to $0.12 per square foot per month
- **Warehouse/industrial cleaning**: $0.03 to $0.08 per square foot per month
- **School/educational facility cleaning**: $0.07 to $0.14 per square foot per month

These ranges vary significantly by region, frequency, and scope. A 20,000-square-foot office in San Francisco commands very different rates than the same size office in Des Moines.

### The Production Rate Method

Production rate is the number of square feet a single cleaner can clean per hour. This is how you translate square footage into labor hours — and labor hours are where your real costs live.

Typical production rates by facility type:

- **Standard office (light cleaning):** 4,000 to 6,000 square feet per hour
- **Standard office (detail cleaning):** 2,500 to 3,500 square feet per hour
- **Medical facility:** 1,500 to 2,500 square feet per hour
- **Retail space:** 3,500 to 5,500 square feet per hour
- **Warehouse:** 8,000 to 15,000 square feet per hour
- **Restrooms:** 500 to 800 square feet per hour

To calculate your bid using production rates:

1. Divide total cleanable square footage by your production rate to get hours per visit.
2. Multiply hours per visit by your fully burdened labor cost (wage plus employer taxes, workers' comp, insurance, benefits).
3. Add supply costs (typically $0.01 to $0.03 per square foot per visit).
4. Add equipment depreciation costs.
5. Add overhead allocation (rent, business insurance, vehicle costs, admin labor, software).
6. Add your profit margin (10% to 20% for competitive bids, 20% to 30% for specialized work).

**Example:** A 15,000-square-foot office, cleaned five nights per week.

- Production rate: 4,000 sq ft/hour = 3.75 hours per visit
- Labor cost: $22/hour fully burdened = $82.50 per visit
- Supplies: $0.02/sq ft = $30 per visit
- Equipment depreciation: $5 per visit
- Overhead allocation: $15 per visit
- Subtotal: $132.50 per visit
- Profit margin (15%): $19.88
- Total per visit: $152.38
- Monthly (22 cleaning days): $3,352
- Per square foot per month: $0.22

Run these numbers through your [financial tracking tools](/product/finance) before you submit. Make sure the margin holds up against your actual overhead, not your assumed overhead.

### Consumables: Include or Exclude?

Consumables include paper towels, toilet paper, hand soap, trash liners, and air fresheners. You have two options:

**Exclude consumables** and let the client supply their own. This simplifies your pricing and removes a variable cost that fluctuates with usage. Most larger facilities prefer this because they have bulk purchasing agreements with suppliers like Grainger or Uline.

**Include consumables** and mark them up 15% to 25%. This gives the client one vendor for everything and increases your per-contract revenue. Smaller facilities — medical offices, small retail stores — often prefer this for convenience. If you include consumables, use a separate line item in your proposal so the client can see the breakdown and so you can adjust the consumable price independently when supplier costs change.

## Writing the Scope of Work

The scope of work (SOW) is the most important document in your commercial cleaning contract. It defines exactly what you will do, how often, and to what standard. A vague scope of work leads to scope creep, client dissatisfaction, and margin erosion. Every dollar you lose to scope creep is a dollar you voluntarily gave away.

### Structure Your SOW Like This

**Daily tasks.** List every task performed on each visit: empty all trash receptacles and replace liners, vacuum all carpeted areas including edges, dust mop and wet mop all hard floors, sanitize all restroom fixtures (toilets, urinals, sinks, countertops, mirrors, dispensers, partitions, door handles), restock restroom consumables, wipe and sanitize kitchen counters and sink, clean break room table surfaces, spot-clean entry glass and high-touch surfaces.

**Weekly tasks.** Tasks done once per week: dust all horizontal surfaces above desk height (shelves, ledges, window sills), clean all interior glass and partitions, vacuum upholstered furniture in common areas, detail-clean kitchen appliances (exterior), dust and wipe all phone and computer surfaces in common areas.

**Monthly tasks.** Deep cleaning tasks: high dusting of vents, diffusers, and light fixtures, baseboard cleaning throughout the facility, carpet spot treatment in high-traffic areas, thorough cleaning of cabinet fronts and shelf surfaces, clean and polish elevator interiors if applicable.

**Quarterly tasks.** Floor care (strip and wax VCT floors, hot water extraction for carpets), deep window cleaning (interior), detail cleaning of light fixtures, deep restroom cleaning including grout scrubbing.

**Annual tasks.** Full facility carpet cleaning, comprehensive high dusting above 10 feet, VCT floor refinishing, exterior window cleaning if included.

### Be Specific About What Is Not Included

This is just as important as listing what is included. If you do not clean exterior windows, say so explicitly. If you do not move furniture to vacuum underneath, state it. If biohazard cleanup (blood, bodily fluids) requires an additional service call at a separate rate, specify the protocol and the hourly rate for emergency callouts. If pressure washing the parking garage is not included, put it in writing.

Ambiguity in a SOW always costs you money. The client will always interpret ambiguity in their favor, and you will eat the cost to preserve the relationship.

## Contract Terms That Protect Your Business

The actual contract wraps around your scope of work and adds the legal and business terms. Get these wrong and a profitable job becomes a liability.

### Contract Duration

Most commercial cleaning contracts run for one year with automatic renewal clauses. Shorter contracts (month-to-month) give the client flexibility but give you no security — you could invest in training, equipment, and startup costs only to lose the account 60 days in. Longer contracts (two to three years) are ideal for your cash flow but harder to negotiate unless you offer a slight discount for the commitment.

Push for a one-year minimum with 30-day cancellation notice after the initial term. This gives both sides protection while providing you enough runway to recoup your startup costs on the account.

### Payment Terms

Standard payment terms for commercial cleaning are Net 15 or Net 30 — meaning the client pays within 15 or 30 days of receiving your invoice. Invoice monthly at the beginning of the month for the current month's service, not at the end. This improves your cash flow by up to 30 days compared to invoicing in arrears.

Late payment is endemic in commercial cleaning. Include a late payment clause with a specific penalty — typically 1.5% per month on overdue balances. More importantly, use [automated invoicing and payment tracking](/product/payments) so you know instantly when an account falls behind and can follow up before it becomes a three-month-old problem.

### Price Escalation Clauses

Include an annual price increase clause — typically 3% to 5% per year, or tied to the Consumer Price Index (CPI). Without this, you are locking yourself into today's prices while your labor costs, insurance premiums, and supply costs rise every year. Most facility managers expect and accept annual increases because they deal with escalation clauses in every vendor contract, not just cleaning.

If you do not include price escalation, you are voluntarily cutting your margin by 3% to 5% every year the contract runs. On a $5,000 per month contract, that is $150 to $250 per month you leave on the table by year two.

### Insurance and Indemnification

Commercial clients will require you to carry general liability insurance (typically $1 million to $2 million per occurrence), workers' compensation insurance, and often a commercial umbrella policy. They will ask to be listed as an additional insured on your general liability policy. This is completely standard — do not push back on it.

Review the indemnification clause carefully or have your attorney review it. You should be willing to indemnify the client for damage caused by your negligence. You should not agree to unlimited indemnification for things outside your control.

### Termination Clauses

Include mutual termination rights with 30 to 60 days written notice. Specify what happens at termination — return of keys and access cards, final invoice terms, removal of your equipment and supplies from the facility. Also include a termination-for-cause clause that allows immediate termination if either party materially breaches the agreement.

## Managing Commercial Accounts After You Win Them

Winning the contract is the beginning, not the end. The first 90 days on a new commercial account set the tone for the entire relationship. Get this wrong and you will spend the rest of the contract in damage-control mode.

### The Startup Phase

During the first two weeks, you and your team should be learning the facility inside and out. Walk every area with your cleaning crew. Identify problem spots — the conference room that is always trashed after Friday meetings, the restroom near the break room that gets twice the traffic, the lobby floor that shows every footprint. Calibrate your time estimates against reality.

Expect your first month to be less profitable than your bid projected. There is a learning curve with every new facility — where the supplies are stored, which doors lock automatically, how to operate the alarm system, which freight elevator to use, where the dumpster is. Build this startup cost into your pricing as a known investment.

### Client Communication Cadence

Set up a communication rhythm from day one:

- **Weekly check-in** (first month): A quick email or text to the facility manager asking if everything meets expectations. Address any issues the same day they are raised.
- **Monthly check-in** (ongoing): A brief report summarizing services performed, any issues encountered, and any recommendations for improvement. Keep it to one page.
- **Quarterly business review** (ongoing): A formal 30-minute sit-down meeting to review the scope of work, discuss performance data, address any needed adjustments, and reinforce the relationship. Bring printed inspection reports and satisfaction data. This meeting is also your opportunity to discuss upsell services.

### Quality Control for Commercial Accounts

Here is the uncomfortable truth about commercial clients: they do not complain like residential clients do. A homeowner calls you the same day if the bathroom was not cleaned properly. A facility manager may silently grow frustrated for months, document every missed spot in a file, and then put the contract out to bid without giving you a chance to fix anything.

Prevent this by proactively auditing your own work. Conduct random inspections on your commercial accounts at least twice per month. Use a standardized checklist tied to your SOW, score the results objectively, and share them with the client proactively. When a facility manager receives an inspection report from you before they have a chance to complain, it builds enormous trust. It tells them you care about quality as much as they do.

### Handling Scope Creep

Scope creep is the silent profit killer in commercial cleaning. It starts small — "Can your team also wipe down the conference table before the Monday morning meeting?" or "Can they take out the recycling bins too?" Each individual request seems trivial, but they add up to hours of unpaid labor per month.

The fix is simple: refer to the scope of work. When a client asks for something outside the SOW, respond professionally: "We are happy to add that to our service. Let me put together a change order with the additional cost." Some clients will decide the extra task is not worth paying for. Others will approve the change order. Either way, you are not giving away free labor.

Document every change order in writing and update the SOW accordingly. This protects both you and the client.

### Upselling Existing Accounts

Your best commercial sales opportunity is the client you already have. Once you have established trust and demonstrated quality, you are in a strong position to sell additional services:

- **Floor care programs** (strip and wax, burnishing, carpet extraction) as quarterly add-ons.
- **Window cleaning** on a quarterly or biannual schedule.
- **Day porter services** for facilities that need daytime cleaning and restocking support.
- **Specialty cleaning** such as high dusting, pressure washing, or post-event cleanup.
- **Expanded scope** to additional floors, buildings, or areas within the same facility.

Time your upsell conversations with your quarterly reviews. Come prepared with specific recommendations based on what you have observed during your regular cleaning. "I have noticed the VCT in the main corridor is showing wear and dulling despite our regular maintenance. A quarterly strip-and-wax program would keep it looking professional and extend the life of the flooring by three to five years. Here is what that would cost."

## Scaling Your Commercial Cleaning Operation

Once you have a few commercial contracts running smoothly, the playbook for scaling is straightforward: systematize everything, hire ahead of demand, and target progressively larger accounts.

### Build Systems Before You Need Them

Every new commercial account should slot into your existing operational system without requiring you to personally manage every detail. That means standardized training for every facility type, SOPs that new cleaners can follow on day one, quality audit processes that run whether you are present or not, and [automated payment collection](/product/payments) so you are not chasing invoices while trying to sell new business.

The companies that scale successfully in commercial cleaning are not the ones with the best sales pitch. They are the ones with the best operations. A prospect who tours your current accounts and sees consistent, documented, audited quality will sign faster than any sales presentation could accomplish.

### Know Your Numbers

Track these metrics for every commercial account, every month:

- **Revenue per labor hour.** Divide monthly contract revenue by total labor hours on the account. Target $35 to $55 per labor hour for standard office cleaning. Below $30, the account is probably unprofitable.
- **Gross margin per account.** After direct labor, supplies, and equipment costs. Target 25% to 35% for healthy commercial accounts. Below 20%, investigate immediately.
- **Client satisfaction score.** From your quarterly reviews, inspection reports, and any feedback received.
- **Contract renewal rate.** What percentage of your contracts renew at the end of the term? The industry average is around 80%. Top companies hit 90% or higher. If you are below 75%, you have a service quality or relationship problem.

Use your [financial management tools](/product/finance) to track profitability per account monthly. If any account falls below your margin target for two consecutive months, investigate and act. Either the scope has crept, the labor estimate was wrong, or there is an efficiency problem on-site that needs solving.

## From Residential to Commercial: Making the Transition

If you are currently running a residential cleaning business and considering the move into commercial, here is the honest reality. Commercial cleaning is a different business with different sales cycles, different client expectations, different staffing needs, and different financial dynamics.

The skills that make you great at residential cleaning — attention to detail, client relationships, flexibility — still matter. But commercial cleaning adds operational complexity that requires better systems, more staff, and a willingness to invest in equipment and insurance before the revenue arrives.

Start with one small [commercial account](/commercial). Target a single office suite, a small medical practice, or a retail store — something between 2,000 and 10,000 square feet. Learn the operational realities of commercial work on a manageable scale before you bid on a 50,000-square-foot office tower. Use that first account to build your SOPs, test your staffing model, develop your quality control process, and learn how commercial billing and collections actually work.

Then scale from a position of competence rather than hope.

Commercial cleaning can transform your business. A handful of well-managed commercial contracts replaces dozens of residential clients and provides the stable, recurring revenue that makes long-term planning, equipment investments, and confident hiring decisions possible. But it demands operational discipline that you need to build deliberately — one walkthrough, one bid, one contract at a time.
