# Cleaning Business Profit Margins: What the Numbers Actually Look Like

Ask ten cleaning business owners what their profit margin is and you will get ten different answers — most of them wrong. Not because they are lying, but because they are not tracking the right numbers. They know what comes in and roughly what goes out, but the gap between "roughly" and "precisely" is where profit margins live and die.

This guide breaks down actual profit margins for cleaning businesses by service type, shows you where the money goes, identifies the biggest margin killers, and gives you concrete strategies to improve your numbers. No motivational fluff. Just the financials.

> **Key Takeaway:**
> - Average net profit margins for cleaning businesses range from 10% to 28% depending on service type and operational efficiency
> - Labor is your largest cost at 45–55% of revenue, followed by supplies (5–10%) and overhead (15–25%)
> - Solo operators typically achieve higher margins (25–35%) than multi-crew businesses (10–20%) but hit a revenue ceiling
> - The difference between a 15% margin and a 25% margin on $300,000 in revenue is $30,000 per year — the equivalent of a part-time salary
> - Pricing strategy has more impact on margins than cost-cutting in nearly every scenario

## What Is a Good Profit Margin for a Cleaning Business?

Before diving into the numbers, let us define terms. There are three types of profit margin that matter.

**[Gross profit margin](/glossary/gross-margin)** is your revenue minus your direct costs (labor, supplies, equipment costs directly tied to performing the service). This tells you how much you make on each job before accounting for overhead.

**Operating profit margin** (also called EBITDA margin) is your revenue minus all operating costs — direct costs plus overhead like insurance, vehicle costs, software, marketing, phone, and office expenses. This tells you how much the business generates before taxes and debt service.

**[Net profit margin](/glossary/net-margin)** is what is left after everything — operating costs, taxes, loan payments, and any other expenses. This is your actual take-home as the business owner (or what you reinvest).

Most cleaning business owners talk about margin without specifying which one, which creates confusion. In this guide, when we say "profit margin" without qualification, we mean **net profit margin** — the money that actually ends up in your pocket.

### Industry Benchmarks by Business Type

| Business Type | Gross Margin | Operating Margin | Net Margin |
|---|---|---|---|
| Solo operator (residential) | 65–75% | 30–40% | 25–35% |
| Small team (2–5 cleaners, residential) | 45–55% | 18–28% | 12–22% |
| Mid-size company (6–20 cleaners) | 40–50% | 15–22% | 10–18% |
| Large company (20+ cleaners) | 35–45% | 12–18% | 8–15% |
| Commercial janitorial | 30–40% | 12–20% | 8–15% |
| Specialized (window, carpet, pressure washing) | 50–65% | 25–35% | 20–30% |

These ranges are wide because margin depends heavily on your market, pricing, efficiency, and cost structure. But they give you a realistic framework for where your business should fall.

**The solo operator paradox** is worth noting. Solo operators have the highest margins in percentage terms but the lowest revenue in absolute terms. A solo cleaner earning $80,000 per year at a 30% net margin takes home $24,000 in profit. A 15-person company earning $600,000 at a 15% net margin takes home $90,000. Lower margin, higher income. The goal is to maintain strong margins as you scale, not to stay solo forever because the percentages look good.

## Profit Margins by Service Type

Different cleaning services carry very different margin profiles. Understanding this is essential for deciding which services to offer and how to allocate your time.

### Residential Standard Cleaning

**Typical net margin: 12–22%**

Standard residential cleaning — regular weekly or biweekly maintenance cleaning of homes — is the bread and butter of most cleaning businesses. It generates consistent, recurring revenue, but margins are moderate because of intense competition and price sensitivity.

**Revenue breakdown for a typical $180 residential clean (3-bedroom home, 2.5 hours, two-person team):**

| Cost Category | Amount | % of Revenue |
|---|---|---|
| Revenue | $180 | 100% |
| Labor (2 cleaners x 2.5 hrs x $17/hr) | $85 | 47.2% |
| Payroll taxes and benefits | $17 | 9.4% |
| Cleaning supplies | $8 | 4.4% |
| Travel/fuel | $12 | 6.7% |
| Insurance (allocated) | $5 | 2.8% |
| Vehicle costs (allocated) | $7 | 3.9% |
| Software/admin (allocated) | $4 | 2.2% |
| Marketing (allocated) | $9 | 5.0% |
| **Total costs** | **$147** | **81.7%** |
| **Net profit** | **$33** | **18.3%** |

The numbers look reasonable in isolation, but consider the sensitivity. If that job takes 3 hours instead of 2.5 (because the house is dirtier than expected, a team member is slower, or there is a long drive), your labor cost jumps to $102 and your margin drops to 11.7%. If you have to redo part of the clean because of a quality complaint, margin drops further. This is why accurate estimating and consistent execution are critical.

### Residential Deep Cleaning

**Typical net margin: 20–30%**

Deep cleans command significantly higher prices than standard cleans because clients understand they require more time, effort, and supplies. The margin is higher because the price premium more than offsets the additional costs.

**Revenue breakdown for a typical $350 deep clean (3-bedroom home, 5 hours, two-person team):**

| Cost Category | Amount | % of Revenue |
|---|---|---|
| Revenue | $350 | 100% |
| Labor (2 cleaners x 5 hrs x $17/hr) | $170 | 48.6% |
| Payroll taxes and benefits | $34 | 9.7% |
| Cleaning supplies (higher for deep cleans) | $18 | 5.1% |
| Travel/fuel | $12 | 3.4% |
| Insurance (allocated) | $5 | 1.4% |
| Vehicle costs (allocated) | $7 | 2.0% |
| Software/admin (allocated) | $4 | 1.1% |
| Marketing (allocated) | $9 | 2.6% |
| **Total costs** | **$259** | **74.0%** |
| **Net profit** | **$91** | **26.0%** |

Deep cleans are more profitable per job because fixed costs (travel, insurance, vehicle, admin) are spread over a higher revenue amount. The labor-to-revenue ratio is similar, but everything else shrinks as a percentage.

**Strategic implication:** Every recurring client should start with a deep clean. This serves two purposes — it gets the property to your maintenance standard and it generates a high-margin job on day one. Never skip the initial deep clean to win a client. You are giving away your most profitable service and making the first standard clean harder.

### Move-In/Move-Out Cleaning

**Typical net margin: 22–32%**

Move-in/move-out cleans are among the most profitable standard services because they are time-sensitive (the client needs it done by a specific date), specialized (empty homes clean differently than furnished ones), and often come with add-on services like oven cleaning, window cleaning, and carpet shampooing.

Clients booking move-out cleans are motivated by lease deposits or sale closings, which makes them less price-sensitive than regular cleaning clients. They need the job done right and done on time, and they will pay for that certainty.

**Key margin drivers:**
- Higher base price ($300–$650 for a 3-bedroom home)
- Add-on services that carry 40–60% margins (oven cleaning, inside windows, carpet treatment)
- No ongoing commitment means no frequency discounts
- Time pressure reduces comparison shopping by clients

### Commercial Janitorial

**Typical net margin: 8–15%**

Commercial cleaning operates on thinner margins than residential, but the trade-off is scale and predictability. A single commercial contract can generate $2,000–$10,000+ per month in recurring revenue with a 12–24 month commitment.

**Revenue breakdown for a typical $3,500/month office cleaning contract (10,000 sq ft, 5 nights per week):**

| Cost Category | Monthly Amount | % of Revenue |
|---|---|---|
| Revenue | $3,500 | 100% |
| Labor (1 cleaner x 3 hrs x 22 nights x $16/hr) | $1,056 | 30.2% |
| Payroll taxes and benefits | $211 | 6.0% |
| Supplies | $175 | 5.0% |
| Equipment depreciation | $85 | 2.4% |
| Insurance | $120 | 3.4% |
| Vehicle costs | $140 | 4.0% |
| Supervision/quality control | $250 | 7.1% |
| Software/admin | $80 | 2.3% |
| Marketing/bid costs (allocated) | $105 | 3.0% |
| Account management | $150 | 4.3% |
| **Total costs** | **$2,372** | **67.8%** |
| **Pretax profit** | **$1,128** | **32.2%** |
| Taxes (estimated) | $280 | 8.0% |
| **Net profit** | **$848** | **24.2%** |

Wait — 24% net margin looks great. Why did we say 8–15% for commercial?

Because the example above is an efficiently run contract. In practice, commercial margins get eaten by several factors that do not affect residential as heavily:

- **Bid pressure.** Commercial contracts go out to bid, and competitors undercut each other. Winning bids often comes with margin compression
- **Scope creep.** Commercial clients gradually expand their expectations without corresponding price increases. The quarterly floor stripping that was not in the original contract somehow becomes your problem
- **Staffing challenges.** Night-shift cleaning positions are harder to fill and have higher turnover, driving up recruitment and training costs
- **Payment terms.** Commercial clients often pay on 30–60 day terms, which creates cash flow challenges

The companies that maintain 15%+ margins on commercial contracts do so through tight scope management, annual price escalation clauses, and efficient operations. ### Specialized Services

**Typical net margins:**

| Service | Net Margin Range |
|---|---|
| Window cleaning | 25–35% |
| Carpet cleaning | 20–30% |
| Pressure washing | 28–40% |
| Post-construction cleaning | 20–30% |
| Biohazard/trauma cleanup | 35–50% |
| Airbnb turnover cleaning | 18–28% |

Specialized services consistently deliver higher margins than general cleaning for several reasons.

**Equipment creates a barrier to entry.** A window cleaning setup costs $2,000–$8,000. A pressure washing rig costs $3,000–$15,000. Carpet cleaning equipment costs $5,000–$25,000. These upfront costs prevent casual competitors from entering the market, which reduces pricing pressure.

**Expertise commands a premium.** Clients are willing to pay more for specialized knowledge. A general cleaner charging $35/hour faces stiff competition. A window cleaner charging $65/hour faces much less, because fewer people have the skills and equipment to compete.

**Perceived risk is higher.** Pressure washing a deck incorrectly can destroy it. Improperly cleaning carpets can cause mold or damage fibers. Clients pay more when they perceive that the job requires skill and that getting it wrong has consequences.

## The Complete Cost Breakdown

Understanding where your money goes is the first step to improving margins. Here is the full cost structure of a typical cleaning business.

### Labor: 45–55% of Revenue

Labor is your largest expense and the one with the most variables. The headline wage you pay your cleaners is only part of the story.

**True labor cost per employee hour (US example):**

| Component | Amount | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Base wage | $17.00 | Varies by market |
| FICA (employer portion, 7.65%) | $1.30 | Social Security + Medicare |
| Workers' compensation insurance | $0.85 | Varies by state, $2–$6 per $100 of payroll |
| Unemployment insurance (FUTA + SUTA) | $0.55 | Varies significantly by state |
| Health insurance (if offered) | $1.50 | Pro-rated per hour |
| Paid time off | $0.65 | If you offer PTO |
| Training time (allocated) | $0.45 | Onboarding + ongoing |
| **True cost per hour** | **$22.30** | **31% above base wage** |

That 31% markup above the base wage is what kills margins for business owners who only account for the headline pay rate. If you are paying $17/hour and charging $35/hour, you might think you are making $18/hour in gross profit. But your true labor cost is $22.30/hour, which means your real gross profit is $12.70/hour — a 30% difference.

Track your true labor cost per hour, not just wages. Every pricing decision, every quote, and every [profit margin calculation](/tools/profit-margin-calculator) should use the fully loaded number.

### Supplies: 5–10% of Revenue

Cleaning supplies are a relatively small line item, but small percentages add up over thousands of jobs.

- **General cleaning chemicals:** $3–$5 per standard residential clean
- **Specialized products (deep cleans):** $8–$15 per job
- **Paper products and disposables:** $2–$4 per job
- **Equipment wear and replacement:** $1–$3 per job (allocated)

**Margin tip:** Buy supplies in bulk from janitorial distributors, not retail stores. The price difference is typically 30–50%. A gallon of all-purpose cleaner from a janitorial supplier costs $8–$12. The equivalent volume from a retail store costs $15–$25. Over 1,000 jobs per year, this saves $3,000–$7,000.

### Overhead: 15–25% of Revenue

Overhead encompasses everything that is not directly tied to performing a specific cleaning job.

| Overhead Item | Monthly Cost (Typical) | Annual Cost |
|---|---|---|
| Business insurance (liability, vehicle) | $150–$400 | $1,800–$4,800 |
| Vehicle payment/lease | $300–$600 | $3,600–$7,200 |
| Fuel | $200–$500 | $2,400–$6,000 |
| Vehicle maintenance | $75–$200 | $900–$2,400 |
| Software subscriptions | $50–$200 | $600–$2,400 |
| Phone/communication | $50–$100 | $600–$1,200 |
| Marketing/advertising | $200–$800 | $2,400–$9,600 |
| Accounting/bookkeeping | $100–$300 | $1,200–$3,600 |
| Office supplies/misc | $25–$75 | $300–$900 |
| **Total overhead** | **$1,150–$3,175** | **$13,800–$38,100** |

For a business doing $250,000 in annual revenue, $13,800–$38,100 in overhead represents 5.5–15.2% of revenue. The range is wide because some businesses run lean (home office, one vehicle, minimal marketing) while others carry higher fixed costs (commercial office, fleet vehicles, significant ad spend).

### The Hidden Costs That Kill Margins

Beyond the standard cost categories, several hidden costs erode margins without showing up on a typical P&L.

**Unbilled travel time.** If your cleaners spend 30 minutes driving between jobs and you do not bill for it, you are giving away 30 minutes of labor cost on every transition. For a team doing 4 jobs per day with 3 transitions, that is 1.5 hours of unbilled labor — $33.45 in true cost per day, or $8,700 per year per team.

**Recleans and callbacks.** Every time you have to send a team back to fix a quality issue, you are performing a job for zero revenue. If 3% of your jobs require a callback and the average callback takes 45 minutes, a business doing 2,000 jobs per year loses 90 hours of productive time — $2,007 in labor cost plus the fuel and disruption cost.

**Employee turnover.** As covered in our [industry statistics](/blog/cleaning-industry-statistics), the cost to replace a cleaning employee is $3,500–$5,500. If you turn over 3 employees per year, that is $10,500–$16,500 in hidden costs — recruitment, training, lost productivity, and client disruption.

**Underpriced jobs.** If 20% of your jobs are underpriced by 15% (because you quoted wrong, gave an unnecessary discount, or did not account for scope changes), you are losing 3% of your total revenue. On $300,000, that is $9,000 per year in margin you should have captured.

## How to Improve Your Profit Margins

Now for the actionable part. Here are the strategies that have the biggest impact on cleaning business margins, ranked by potential impact.

### 1. Fix Your Pricing (Impact: 5–15% Margin Improvement)

Pricing has more impact on margins than any other single factor. A 10% price increase on $300,000 in revenue adds $30,000 to the top line — and because your costs do not increase proportionally, most of that drops straight to the bottom line.

**Calculate your true cost per job.** Use the cost categories above to determine what every job actually costs you. Then add your target profit margin on top. Our guide on [how to price cleaning services](/blog/how-to-price-cleaning-services) walks through this process in detail. If you are not covering all your costs in your pricing, no amount of cost-cutting will save you.

**Eliminate underpriced jobs.** Review your client list and identify jobs where you are earning below your target margin. These are the jobs that feel like a grind — they are a grind because you are not making money on them. Raise prices or let them go. Use our [pricing calculator](/tools/pricing-calculator) to model the impact.

**Price deep cleans and first cleans correctly.** Your initial clean for any new client should be priced as a deep clean, not a standard clean. This is the most common pricing mistake in residential cleaning. The first visit always takes longer and requires more supplies. Price accordingly.

**Add an annual price increase.** Costs rise every year — wages, fuel, insurance, supplies. If your prices stay flat, your margins shrink by 3–5% annually through inflation alone. Build a 3–5% annual increase into your client agreements and communicate it proactively.

> **The Math on Price Increases**
>
> A cleaning business doing $300,000 annually with a 15% net margin earns $45,000 in profit. A 10% price increase (assuming you lose 5% of clients who refuse the increase) yields approximately $28,500 in additional revenue — most of which is pure profit because your costs barely change. Your new margin: approximately 22%. That is a 47% increase in take-home pay from a single pricing decision.

### 2. Reduce Employee Turnover (Impact: 3–8% Margin Improvement)

Turnover is a margin destroyer. Every time you lose an employee, you spend $3,500–$5,500 to replace them, and you lose productivity during the training period. Cutting your annual turnover from 200% to 100% at a 10-person company saves $35,000–$55,000 per year.

**Pay above market.** The most effective retention tool is also the most obvious. If the going rate in your market is $15/hour, pay $17. The $2/hour premium costs $4,160 per year per full-time employee. The cost of replacing that employee is $3,500–$5,500. You come out ahead on the first retention alone, and every additional year of tenure is pure savings.

**Create predictable schedules.** As we cover in our [scheduling guide](/blog/cleaning-business-scheduling-guide), cleaning employees frequently cite schedule unpredictability as a primary reason for leaving. Use [scheduling software](/product/scheduling) to create consistent weekly schedules and give employees visibility into their upcoming shifts.

**Offer performance bonuses.** A $50–$100 monthly bonus for zero callbacks and perfect attendance costs $600–$1,200 per year and pays for itself by reducing quality issues and absenteeism.

### 3. Optimize Routing and Scheduling (Impact: 2–5% Margin Improvement)

Windshield time — the time your teams spend driving between jobs — is pure cost with zero revenue. Reducing drive time by 15–20 minutes per day per team adds up fast.

**Cluster jobs geographically.** Schedule jobs in the same neighborhood on the same day. This seems obvious, but many cleaning businesses schedule based on client preference alone, resulting in zigzag routes that waste fuel and time.

**Minimize transition time.** The ideal schedule has each team working in a tight geographic area with no more than 10–15 minutes between jobs. Every minute saved in transit is a minute that can be billed.

**Build buffer into your schedule.** A schedule with zero slack looks efficient on paper but falls apart with one late start or one job that runs long. Build 15–30 minutes of buffer per day. You will still come out ahead because the alternative — running late, rushing through jobs, and creating callbacks — costs more than the buffer time.

### 4. Systematize Quality Control (Impact: 1–3% Margin Improvement)

Callbacks and recleans cost money in labor, fuel, and client goodwill. Reducing them from 3–5% of jobs to under 1% has a direct margin impact.

**Create cleaning checklists.** A standardized checklist ensures consistency and gives team members a clear reference for what "done" looks like. Use [custom forms](/product/custom-forms) to build digital checklists that teams complete on their phones.

**Implement before/after photos.** Require teams to take photos of key areas before and after cleaning. This creates accountability, provides documentation if a client disputes quality, and helps you identify training needs.

**Random quality audits.** Visit a random selection of jobs each week after the team has finished. Catching issues proactively is far cheaper than waiting for client complaints.

### 5. Upsell Add-On Services (Impact: 2–5% Margin Improvement)

Add-on services — oven cleaning, fridge cleaning, inside windows, laundry, organizing — carry higher margins than base cleaning because the incremental cost is low (you are already at the property) while the incremental revenue is significant.

**The math is compelling.** If 30% of your clients add an average of $35 in add-on services per visit, and you serve 150 recurring clients biweekly, that is $40,950 in annual revenue from add-ons. The incremental cost is approximately 30–40 minutes of additional labor per job, making the margin on add-ons 40–60%.

**How to sell add-ons without being pushy:**
- Include a menu of add-on services in your booking confirmation
- Train cleaners to mention one relevant add-on at each visit ("I noticed your oven could use some attention — want me to add that on today?")
- Offer seasonal promotions (spring deep clean add-ons, holiday prep services)
- Use your [invoicing system](/product/payments) to make add-on ordering easy

### 6. Track Your Numbers Religiously (Impact: Foundational)

You cannot improve what you do not measure. At minimum, track these metrics monthly:

- **Revenue per cleaning hour** — total revenue divided by total hours spent cleaning (not total hours worked). Target: 2.5x–3.5x your fully loaded labor cost
- **Gross margin per job** — revenue minus direct costs for each individual job. Flag any job below your target margin for review
- **Callback rate** — percentage of jobs requiring a return visit. Target: under 1%
- **Client retention rate** — percentage of recurring clients who remain active month over month. Target: 95%+ monthly, which translates to 54%+ annual retention
- **Revenue per employee** — total revenue divided by number of cleaning staff. Target: $50,000–$75,000 per full-time equivalent

Use [financial tracking and reporting tools](/product/finance) to track these metrics automatically rather than manually. Manual tracking is better than nothing, but it is inconsistent and time-consuming. Automated tracking through your business software gives you real-time visibility into your financial health.

## Common Margin Mistakes

These are the patterns we see repeatedly in cleaning businesses that struggle with profitability.

**Not paying yourself a market-rate salary.** Many business owners exclude their own compensation from their cost calculations, making their margins look artificially high. If you are cleaning 30 hours a week and doing 15 hours of admin, you should be paying yourself for all 45 hours at a rate that reflects your skills and market. If your business cannot afford to pay you a fair wage and still be profitable, your pricing is wrong.

**Discounting to fill empty schedule slots.** This trains clients to expect discounts and attracts price-shoppers who will leave the moment they find someone cheaper. If your schedule has empty slots, the solution is better marketing — not lower prices.

**Ignoring the cost of your time on admin.** Every hour you spend quoting, scheduling, invoicing, and following up on payments is an hour you could spend cleaning (revenue-generating) or marketing (growth-generating). If admin consumes 15 hours per week at an opportunity cost of $40/hour, that is $31,200 per year in hidden cost. Invest in [business management software](/product) that automates the admin and reclaim those hours.

**Growing revenue without watching margins.** Revenue growth feels good, but revenue growth with declining margins means more work for the same or less profit. Every time you add a new client, a new employee, or a new service, check the impact on your margins. Growth should be profitable, not just busy.

**Failing to raise prices annually.** This is the slow bleed that catches most businesses. Costs increase 3–5% annually through inflation, wage growth, and insurance increases. If your prices stay flat, you are taking a 3–5% pay cut every year. Build annual increases into your client agreements and communicate them clearly.

## Building a Profitable Cleaning Business: The Financial Roadmap

Here is what a healthy financial trajectory looks like for a cleaning business from startup through maturity.

**Year 1 (Solo operator):**
- Revenue: $50,000–$80,000
- Net margin: 25–35%
- Take-home: $12,500–$28,000
- Focus: Building client base, establishing systems, learning your market

**Year 2 (First employee):**
- Revenue: $100,000–$160,000
- Net margin: 15–22%
- Take-home: $15,000–$35,200
- Focus: Hiring right, training, maintaining quality with a team

**Year 3 (Small team, 3–5 cleaners):**
- Revenue: $180,000–$350,000
- Net margin: 12–20%
- Take-home: $21,600–$70,000
- Focus: Systems, processes, route efficiency, reducing owner dependency

**Year 4–5 (Established operation, 6–15 cleaners):**
- Revenue: $350,000–$750,000
- Net margin: 15–22%
- Take-home: $52,500–$165,000
- Focus: Management structure, multiple crews, commercial contracts, brand building

Notice the margin dip in Year 2. This is normal and expected. When you go from solo operator to employer, your costs increase faster than your revenue initially. The transition period is financially uncomfortable but necessary for scaling beyond the solo ceiling.

The key metric to watch during scaling is not your margin percentage — it is your absolute take-home. A 15% margin on $400,000 ($60,000) is better than a 30% margin on $80,000 ($24,000), even though the percentage is half. The goal is to grow revenue while maintaining margins in a healthy range, not to maximize the percentage at the expense of growth.

## The Bottom Line on Profit Margins

Profit margin is not a vanity metric. It is the number that tells you whether your business is working. Every pricing decision, every hire, every process change, and every technology investment should be evaluated through the lens of margin impact.

The cleaning businesses that consistently deliver 18–25%+ net margins share common characteristics: they price based on costs rather than competitors, they invest in systems that reduce waste and callbacks, they track their numbers obsessively, and they raise prices annually.

If you are not where you want to be on margins, start with pricing. It is the highest-impact lever available to you and the one that most cleaning business owners underuse. Run your numbers through the [profit margin calculator](/tools/profit-margin-calculator), identify your biggest margin gaps, and address them systematically.

Your margins will not fix themselves. But with the right data and the right strategy, you can fix them — and the difference between average and excellent margins is the difference between a cleaning job and a cleaning business.
