# How to Start a Cleaning Business: The No-BS Guide

You are reading this because you are thinking about starting a cleaning business, or you already started one and realized you skipped some important steps. Either way, this guide is built for you. No motivational fluff, no "follow your passion" platitudes, no generic advice that applies to every business from food trucks to SaaS companies. Just the specific, tactical, numbers-driven steps you need to go from zero to a functioning, profitable cleaning operation.

Starting a cleaning business is one of the most accessible paths to self-employment. The startup costs are low, the demand is constant regardless of economic cycles, and you do not need a degree, certification, or five years of experience to get your first client. But accessible does not mean easy. The cleaning industry is competitive, margins can be thin if you price wrong, and most new cleaning businesses fail within the first two years because of avoidable mistakes.

This guide will help you avoid every single one of those mistakes.

## Step 1: Legal Setup — Do This Before You Clean a Single House

Skipping the legal foundation is the most common and most costly mistake new cleaning business owners make. It feels like unnecessary bureaucracy when you are eager to start earning, but one incident without proper coverage can end your business and create personal financial liability that follows you for years.

### Choose Your Business Structure

You have three realistic options:

**Sole Proprietorship.** The simplest and cheapest structure. You file a DBA ("doing business as") with your county, which costs $10 to $50 in most places. The problem is that a sole proprietorship offers zero liability protection. If a client sues you, they can come after your personal bank account, your car, and your home. For a business where you are entering other people's private property and handling chemicals, this is a serious risk.

**Single-Member LLC.** This is the right choice for most new cleaning businesses. An LLC separates your personal assets from your business liabilities. Filing costs range from $50 to $500 depending on your state (California is $70 plus an $800 annual franchise tax; Wyoming is $100 with no annual fee). The liability protection alone is worth the cost. If something goes wrong on a job and a client sues, they can only go after business assets, not your personal savings.

**S-Corp Election.** This is not a separate business type — it is a tax election you can make for your LLC once you are earning $40,000 or more in annual profit. It lets you split your income between salary and distributions, which can save you $3,000 to $8,000 per year in self-employment taxes. But it adds accounting complexity, so wait until your revenue justifies the savings.

### Get Your EIN

Apply for a free Employer Identification Number from the IRS at irs.gov. It takes five minutes online and you get your number immediately. You need it to open a business bank account, file taxes, and eventually hire employees.

### Open a Business Bank Account

This is non-negotiable. Walk into any bank with your LLC paperwork and EIN, and open a business checking account. Never mix personal and business finances. Commingling funds weakens your LLC's liability protection and turns your bookkeeping into a nightmare at tax time.

Choose a bank with free or low-cost business checking, online banking, and integration with accounting software. Many online banks like Relay or Mercury offer free business checking with better technology than traditional banks.

### Register for State and Local Taxes

Depending on your state, you may need to register for state sales tax collection (some states tax cleaning services, some do not), state income tax withholding (once you have employees), and local business licenses or permits. Check with your state's Department of Revenue and your city or county clerk's office.

### Set Aside Money for Taxes From Day One

This is where new business owners get burned. As a self-employed person, nobody is withholding taxes from your income. You owe quarterly estimated tax payments to the IRS and your state. If you skip these payments, you will face penalties on top of the tax bill.

Open a separate savings account and transfer 25% to 30% of every dollar you earn into it immediately. This money is not yours — it belongs to the government. When quarterly taxes come due (April 15, June 15, September 15, January 15), you will have the cash ready instead of scrambling.

## Step 2: Insurance — The Non-Negotiable Foundation

Insurance is not optional for a cleaning business. It is the foundation that protects everything else you build. Get it before your first job, not after.

### General Liability Insurance

This covers property damage and bodily injury that occurs during your work. If you accidentally scratch a client's hardwood floor with your vacuum, knock a vase off a shelf, or a client trips over your mop bucket, general liability insurance covers the claim.

Cost: $30 to $100 per month for a solo operator, depending on your coverage limits and location. Most policies start at $1 million per occurrence and $2 million aggregate. This is adequate for residential cleaning. Commercial clients may require higher limits.

### Workers' Compensation Insurance

Required by law in most states as soon as you have your first employee. It covers medical costs and lost wages if an employee is injured on the job. Cleaning involves repetitive motions, chemical exposure, wet floors, and lifting — injuries happen. Workers' comp costs vary by state but typically run $0.75 to $2.50 per $100 of payroll.

Even if you are a solo operator, some states require workers' comp for yourself. Check your state's requirements.

### Bonding

A surety bond protects your clients against theft by you or your employees. It is not the same as insurance — a bond guarantees that you will compensate the client if theft occurs. Being bonded costs $100 to $300 per year and significantly increases client trust. Many clients will not hire an unbonded cleaning service, especially for recurring work where you will have keys to their home.

### Business Auto Insurance

If you use a personal vehicle for business purposes (driving between jobs, transporting equipment), your personal auto policy may not cover incidents that occur during business use. Talk to your auto insurance agent about adding a business use rider or getting a separate commercial auto policy.

## Step 3: Equipment List — What You Actually Need

You do not need a van full of professional equipment to start. But you do need quality tools that produce professional results. Here is the list, separated into what you need on day one versus what can wait.

### Day One Equipment (Total: $450 to $900)

- **Commercial-grade upright vacuum with HEPA filtration** — $250 to $500. This is your most important tool. Do not buy a consumer-grade vacuum. A commercial Hoover, Sanitaire, or ProTeam will last five times longer and clean significantly better. HEPA filtration is increasingly expected by clients, especially those with allergies or pets.
- **Flat mop system with microfiber pads** — $40 to $80. Flat mops outperform traditional string mops in every way: faster, more sanitary, lighter, and they leave less residual moisture.
- **Microfiber cloths (30 to 50 count)** — $25 to $50. Color-code them: blue for glass and mirrors, green for kitchens, red for bathrooms, yellow for general dusting. This prevents cross-contamination and looks professional to clients.
- **Spray bottles (6 to 8)** — $10 to $15. Label each one with the solution name and dilution ratio.
- **Cleaning caddy or bucket organizer** — $15 to $25. Keeps your supplies organized and mobile as you move room to room.
- **Scrub brushes (assorted sizes)** — $15 to $20. Including a grout brush, a general-purpose scrub brush, and a detail brush for tight spaces.
- **Rubber gloves (multiple pairs)** — $10 to $15. Nitrile or latex, depending on allergies.
- **Telescoping duster** — $15 to $25. For ceiling fans, high shelves, and crown molding.
- **Knee pads** — $15 to $25. You will be on your knees cleaning tubs, baseboards, and floors. Protect them.
- **Squeegee** — $10 to $15. For shower doors, mirrors, and windows.

### Day One Chemicals (Total: $60 to $120)

- All-purpose cleaner concentrate — $8 to $15
- Glass cleaner concentrate — $6 to $10
- Bathroom disinfectant — $8 to $12
- Toilet bowl cleaner — $5 to $8
- Degreaser for kitchen work — $8 to $12
- Hardwood floor cleaner — $8 to $12
- Stainless steel cleaner — $6 to $10
- Trash bags (variety of sizes) — $10 to $20

Buy concentrates from janitorial supply distributors like Zep, Nyco, or your local janitorial supply house. The cost per use is 50% to 75% less than buying retail-sized bottles from the grocery store, and the products are formulated for professional use.

### Equipment to Buy Later (Month 3 to 6)

- Cordless stick vacuum for quick touch-ups — $150 to $300
- Handheld steam cleaner — $80 to $150
- Step stool (two-step) — $30 to $50
- Carpet spot cleaner machine — $100 to $200
- Pumice stone for toilet ring removal — $5

## Step 4: Setting Your Initial Prices

Pricing is where new cleaning businesses either set themselves up for profitability or dig a hole they spend years climbing out of. Get this right from the start.

### The Fatal Mistake: Pricing Based on Competitors

Your instinct will be to check what other cleaners in your area charge and then set your prices slightly lower. This is a trap. Your competitor might have lower insurance costs, might be paying themselves below minimum wage, might be skipping taxes, or might be losing money and does not know it yet. Their prices tell you nothing about what your business needs to charge to be profitable.

### How to Calculate Your Real Minimum Rate

Work backwards from what you need to earn:

1. **Target annual take-home pay:** $50,000 (adjust to your needs)
2. **Add self-employment taxes (15.3%):** $7,650
3. **Add income taxes (estimate 12% effective):** $6,000
4. **Add business expenses (insurance, supplies, gas, software):** $12,000
5. **Total revenue needed:** $75,650
6. **Billable hours per year** (accounting for drive time, admin, marketing — realistically 1,200 to 1,400 hours of actual cleaning): 1,300
7. **Minimum hourly rate:** $58.19 per hour

That is your floor. Anything below that and you are not earning your target income. Now, you will not quote clients an hourly rate — you will quote flat rates per job. But this calculation tells you how to evaluate whether a job is worth taking. If a three-bedroom house will take you 2.5 hours and you quote $135, your effective rate is $54 per hour — below your target. You need to quote $150 or more.

### Residential Pricing Benchmarks for 2026

These are the ranges you should be pricing within for standard recurring cleans:

- **Studio or 1-bedroom apartment:** $90 to $140
- **2-bedroom home (under 1,500 sq ft):** $130 to $190
- **3-bedroom home (1,500 to 2,500 sq ft):** $170 to $260
- **4-bedroom home (2,500+ sq ft):** $220 to $380
- **Initial deep clean:** 1.5x to 2.5x the standard clean price
- **Move-out clean:** 2x to 3x the standard clean price

Price in the middle of these ranges when you are starting out. Once you have 15+ five-star reviews and a waitlist, move to the upper range. Check the [Spotless pricing page](/pricing) for additional tools and frameworks to refine your rates as you grow.

### The Recurring Client Discount

Offer a 10% to 15% discount for clients who commit to weekly or biweekly recurring service. This discount is worth it because recurring clients reduce your marketing costs, provide predictable revenue, and become faster to clean as you learn their home.

A client paying $180 for a biweekly clean at a 10% discount ($162) generates $4,212 per year. A one-time client paying $200 generates $200. Recurring revenue is the foundation of a sustainable cleaning business.

## Step 5: Getting Your First Clients on Zero Budget

You do not need a marketing budget to get your first clients. You need hustle, a phone, and the willingness to tell people what you do.

### Your Personal Network (Clients 1 to 3)

Text or call every person you know and tell them you have started a cleaning business. Not a mass text — personalized messages. "Hey Sarah, I just started a professional cleaning business. If you or anyone you know needs a great cleaning, I would love to earn your business. Here is what I offer." Your first two to three clients will almost certainly come from people who already know and trust you.

### Nextdoor and Local Facebook Groups (Clients 3 to 7)

Join every local Facebook group and Nextdoor neighborhood in your service area. Do not spam your services. Instead, watch for posts from people asking for cleaner recommendations, and respond with a thoughtful, specific offer. "Hi! I just started [Business Name] and I specialize in detailed residential cleaning in [Area]. I am offering 20% off first cleans this month. Happy to send you more details if interested." One good response on a busy Nextdoor post can generate three to five leads.

### Google Business Profile (Long-Term Lead Generation)

Set up your free Google Business Profile the same week you register your business. This is what puts you on Google Maps when someone searches "house cleaning near me." Add photos (before-and-after shots are gold), your service area, your hours, and a compelling business description. Ask every early client for a Google review. The businesses that dominate local search are the ones with the most reviews, not the biggest ad budgets.

### Door Hangers and Flyers (Clients 5 to 10)

Design a simple, professional door hanger and distribute 200 to 500 in your target neighborhoods. Target areas with the demographics of your ideal client — typically neighborhoods with dual-income households, newer homes, or higher property values. Include a first-clean discount and a clear call to action. Response rates are typically 0.5% to 2%, so 500 door hangers should generate 2 to 10 calls.

### Realtor and Property Manager Partnerships

Real estate agents need move-out cleans, move-in cleans, and staging cleans for every property transaction. Property managers need turnover cleans between tenants. Visit every real estate office and property management company in your area. Bring a one-page leave-behind with your services, pricing, and contact information. Offer a $25 referral fee for every client they send you.

One active realtor who sells 20 homes per year can generate 20 to 40 cleaning jobs annually — and many of those one-time move-in clients convert to recurring service.

### Do Work That Markets Itself

The most powerful marketing for a new cleaning business is doing exceptional work and asking clients to tell their friends. When you clean a home, leave it better than the client expected. Clean the light switch plates nobody asked about. Wipe down the front of the washing machine. Leave a handwritten thank-you note on the kitchen counter. These small touches generate word-of-mouth that no ad budget can buy.

## Step 6: Setting Up Operations for Scale From Day One

The biggest operational mistake new cleaning business owners make is starting with paper notebooks, text messages, and mental checklists. You will outgrow these systems within weeks, and migrating later is painful because your data is scattered and your habits are established.

Set up proper systems from the start, even if you are a solo operator. When you grow from one to five to twenty clients, your systems scale with you instead of breaking under the weight.

### What You Need in Your Tech Stack

- **Scheduling and calendar management.** You need a system that shows you (and eventually your staff) where to be, when, with what supplies, and for how long. Not a paper planner — a digital system that sends reminders and tracks changes.
- **Client database.** Every client's address, access instructions, cleaning preferences, pet information, and service history in one searchable place.
- **Invoicing and payment collection.** Send professional invoices and collect payment electronically. Chasing cash and checks slows your cash flow and makes you look unprofessional.
- **Communication automation.** Booking confirmations, appointment reminders, and follow-up messages should send automatically so you are not manually texting 15 clients every week.

## Step 7: Common First-Year Mistakes (and How to Avoid Them)

Every new cleaning business owner makes some of these mistakes. The ones who survive are the ones who recognize and correct them quickly.

### Mistake 1: Underpricing to Get Clients

You set your prices low because you are new and feel like you need to "earn" the right to charge more. Then you attract price-sensitive clients who are the hardest to retain, the most demanding, and the first to leave when someone cheaper appears. Meanwhile, you are working 50-hour weeks and taking home less than you would earn as an employee.

**The fix:** Price for profitability from day one using the calculation in Step 4. If a prospect says you are too expensive, they are not your client. The clients who pay fair prices are the easiest to work with, the most loyal, and the least likely to nickel-and-dime you.

### Mistake 2: Saying Yes to Every Job

A hoarder home that takes seven hours to clean. A client 45 minutes outside your service area. A first-time deep clean on a house that has not been cleaned in two years. These jobs eat your time, drain your energy, and are almost never profitable.

**The fix:** Set clear criteria for the jobs you accept. Define your service area geographically (a maximum drive radius). Set minimum job sizes. Require an initial deep clean before starting recurring service on heavily soiled homes, and price that initial clean appropriately — $400 to $800 for a neglected 3-bedroom home is not unreasonable.

### Mistake 3: Not Getting Reviews

You do great work but never ask for reviews. Meanwhile, your competitor with mediocre quality has 87 Google reviews and gets all the local search traffic.

**The fix:** Ask every single client for a Google review after their first clean. Make it easy — send them the direct link via text. "Hi [Name], thank you for choosing [Business Name]! If you were happy with the clean, would you mind leaving us a quick Google review? Here is the link: [link]. It really helps small businesses like ours." Most happy clients will leave a review if you make the ask and make it simple.

### Mistake 4: No Written Agreements

You agree to clean a home weekly for $160, but three months later the client claims they were quoted $140. Or a client demands you clean their oven, which was never part of the agreed service. Without a written agreement, you have no way to resolve these disputes.

**The fix:** Use a simple service agreement for every client. It does not need to be a complex contract — one page listing the services included, the frequency, the price, the cancellation policy, and the payment terms. Both parties sign it. This protects you and sets clear expectations.

### Mistake 5: Stopping Marketing When You Get Busy

You market aggressively for the first three months, fill your schedule, and stop all marketing activities. Six months later, natural client attrition (moves, budget changes, life events) has created gaps in your schedule, and your pipeline is empty.

**The fix:** Marketing is a constant activity, not a one-time campaign. Dedicate two to three hours per week to marketing even when you are fully booked. Maintain your Google Business Profile, post on social media, nurture realtor relationships, and ask for referrals. A full schedule today does not guarantee a full schedule next quarter.

### Mistake 6: Waiting Too Long to Raise Prices

You started at $140 for a standard three-bedroom clean. A year later, your costs have increased, your skills have improved, and you have 40 five-star reviews — but you are still charging $140 because you are afraid of losing clients.

**The fix:** Raise prices annually, every January or on the anniversary of each client's start date. A 5% to 8% annual increase is standard and expected. Give clients 30 days notice. The vast majority will accept it without question. The few who leave over a $10 increase were not clients you wanted long-term anyway.

### Mistake 7: Trying to Scale Before Systems Exist

You hire your first employee before you have documented how you clean, what products you use, how you interact with clients, or how you handle problems. The new hire does everything differently than you do, clients complain, and you end up re-cleaning their work or losing the client.

**The fix:** Document everything before you hire. Write SOPs for every service type. Create checklists for every room. Record short training videos on your phone. Build the system first, then plug people into it. Your first hire should be able to follow your documented process and deliver 90% of your quality on day one.

## Month-by-Month Roadmap: Your First Six Months

Here is a realistic timeline with specific milestones for each month.

### Month 1: Foundation

- Register your LLC and get your EIN.
- Purchase general liability insurance and bonding.
- Buy your day-one equipment and supplies ($500 to $900).
- Set up your Google Business Profile with photos and service descriptions.
- Build a simple website or booking page.
- Set up cleaning business software for scheduling, invoicing, and client management.
- Activate your personal network — text and call everyone you know.
- **Target: 2 to 4 paying clients.**

### Month 2: Traction

- Refine your cleaning process based on early client feedback.
- Ask every client for a Google review (target: 5+ reviews).
- Post in local Facebook groups and Nextdoor weekly.
- Distribute 300 to 500 door hangers in target neighborhoods.
- Reach out to 5 to 10 realtors and property managers.
- Offer recurring service packages to one-time clients.
- **Target: 6 to 10 active clients.**

### Month 3: Systems

- Write SOPs for your standard clean, deep clean, and move-out clean.
- Set up automated booking confirmations and reminders.
- Establish a consistent invoicing and collection process.
- Start tracking key metrics: revenue per week, average job value, close rate on estimates.
- **Target: 10 to 15 active clients. Evaluate whether you need part-time help.**

### Month 4: Growth

- If demand justifies it, hire your first part-time cleaner.
- Train them for a full week using your SOPs before any solo work.
- Increase marketing spend on channels that are working (track cost per lead by channel).
- Introduce add-on services (inside fridge, inside oven, laundry, organizing) to increase average ticket.
- **Target: 15 to 20 active clients.**

### Month 5: Optimization

- Analyze which clients and job types are most profitable, and focus your marketing on attracting more of those.
- If your estimate close rate is above 70%, your prices are too low — raise them for new clients.
- Launch a referral program: offer existing clients a $25 credit for every referral that books.
- Begin exploring small commercial accounts if residential operations are stable.
- **Target: Consistent weekly revenue that covers all business and personal expenses.**

### Month 6: Scale Preparation

- Document every process in your business so it can operate without your constant presence.
- Evaluate whether to hire additional staff, add a second team, or stay lean and profitable.
- Set specific revenue and profit goals for months 7 through 12.
- Reinvest a portion of profits into better equipment, expanded marketing, or technology upgrades.
- **Target: Clear monthly profitability with a growing pipeline of new leads.**

## The Bottom Line

Starting a cleaning business is straightforward but not simple. The businesses that survive past year one and actually build something meaningful are the ones that treat it as a real business from day one — proper legal structure, adequate insurance, professional pricing, systematic marketing, and technology-driven operations.

You do not need to have everything figured out before you start. But you do need to start right. Register your LLC, get insured, buy your equipment, set up your [technology stack](/product), and go clean your first house. Everything else you will learn by doing, and every section of this guide will be here to reference as each new challenge comes up.

The cleaning industry rewards consistency, reliability, and professionalism above all else. Deliver those three things every day, and you will build a business that supports the life you actually want to live.
