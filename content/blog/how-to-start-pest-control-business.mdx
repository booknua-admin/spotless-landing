# How to Start a Pest Control Business: Complete Guide

Pest control is one of the most recession-resistant service businesses you can start. Bugs do not care about the economy. Rodents do not check stock prices before invading a kitchen. Termites do not stop eating because consumer confidence is low. When pests show up, people pay to make them go away — fast and without negotiation.

The pest control industry generates over $23 billion annually in the United States alone, with steady year-over-year growth driven by climate change expanding pest ranges, urbanization creating new habitat interfaces, and increasing regulatory pressure on food service and healthcare facilities. The average pest control company operates at 15% to 25% net margins once established, which is significantly higher than most service businesses.

But this is not a business you can start by watching a few YouTube videos and buying a sprayer. Pest control involves regulated chemicals, licensing requirements, liability exposure, and a knowledge base that takes time to develop. Get it wrong and you face fines, lawsuits, or worse — harming someone's health. Get it right and you have a business with high recurring revenue, strong margins, and clients who stay for years.

This guide covers every step from zero to operating pest control company, with real numbers, practical advice, and none of the vague motivational filler that wastes your time.

## Understanding the Pest Control Industry

Before you invest a dollar, understand what you are getting into. Pest control is not one business — it is several distinct service lines under one umbrella, each with different equipment needs, chemical knowledge, licensing requirements, and profit margins.

### General Pest Control

This is the bread and butter. Ants, cockroaches, spiders, silverfish, earwigs, centipedes, and other common household pests. General pest control is where most companies start because the treatments are relatively straightforward, the chemicals are widely available, and the licensing requirements are less complex than specialty services.

Typical service: quarterly perimeter and interior treatments using liquid residual insecticides, granular baits, and dust applications. Revenue per residential client runs $400 to $700 per year on quarterly plans.

### Rodent Control

Mice and rats require a different approach than insects. You are dealing with trapping, exclusion work (sealing entry points), bait stations, and ongoing monitoring. Rodent control often overlaps with wildlife control but typically falls under your standard pest control license.

Profit margins on rodent work are excellent because the labor and material costs are low relative to what clients will pay. A mouse exclusion job that takes two hours and costs $30 in materials can easily command $350 to $600.

### Termite Control

Termites are the high-stakes segment. Damage from termites costs US homeowners over $5 billion annually. Treatments include liquid barrier treatments (termiticides applied to soil around the foundation), baiting systems (like Sentricon or Advance), and wood treatments. Many states require a separate termite license or endorsement.

Termite work commands premium pricing — $1,500 to $3,000 for an initial treatment, with annual renewal inspections at $200 to $400. Termite damage repair referral relationships can also be lucrative.

### Bed Bug Treatment

Bed bugs exploded as a problem in the 2000s and have not gone away. Treatment methods include chemical treatments (multiple visits required), heat treatments (entire rooms or structures heated to 120-140 degrees Fahrenheit), and steam treatments. Heat treatment equipment is expensive ($15,000 to $50,000) but commands premium prices — $1,500 to $4,000 per treatment.

### Wildlife and Exclusion

Squirrels, raccoons, bats, birds, and other wildlife in structures. This often requires separate licensing (wildlife control operator permits) and involves trapping, removal, and exclusion work. Not every pest control company offers this, but it is a high-margin add-on.

### Mosquito and Tick Control

Seasonal service in most markets, typically March through October. Involves barrier sprays (synthetic pyrethroids or natural alternatives), larviciding, and habitat modification advice. Recurring monthly service at $75 to $150 per treatment, with 7 to 8 treatments per season generating $525 to $1,200 per client annually.

> **Pro Tip**
Do not try to offer every service from day one. Start with general pest control and rodent control. These two service lines cover 70% to 80% of residential pest complaints and require the least specialized equipment. Add termite, bed bug, and specialty services as you gain experience and capital.

## Licensing and Certification Requirements

This is the most critical step and the one that separates pest control from most other service businesses. You cannot legally apply pesticides commercially without proper licensing.

### State Licensing (United States)

Every state regulates commercial pesticide application through its department of agriculture or environmental protection agency. Requirements vary but typically include the following.

**Applicator License:** You must pass one or more state-administered exams covering general pesticide safety, environmental protection, and pest-specific categories. Most states offer category-specific endorsements.

Common exam categories include:
- General pest control (insects and spiders)
- Termite and wood-destroying organisms
- Rodent control
- Fumigation
- Lawn and ornamental
- Mosquito control
- Public health pest control

**Study materials** are typically available from your state's department of agriculture. Many states use or reference EPA-approved training manuals. Budget 4 to 8 weeks of study time for the general exam, more for specialty categories.

**Business License:** Separate from your applicator license, you need a pest control business license (sometimes called a structural pest control license) to operate a company. Requirements often include proof of insurance, a licensed applicator on staff, and sometimes a physical business location.

**Continuing Education:** Most states require 8 to 20 hours of continuing education per licensing period (typically every 1 to 3 years) to maintain your applicator license. CE courses are available through industry associations, online providers, and state extension offices.

### Certification Programs

Beyond mandatory licensing, voluntary certifications boost credibility and help you win commercial accounts.

**QualityPro Certification** (through the National Pest Management Association) is the gold standard. Requirements include background checks for all employees, a standardized new employee training program, continuing education, and adherence to a code of ethics. Fewer than 3% of pest control companies hold QualityPro certification, making it a significant differentiator.

**ACE Certification** (Associate Certified Entomologist) demonstrates advanced pest identification and management knowledge. Requires passing a comprehensive exam and is particularly valuable for marketing credibility.

**GreenPro Certification** focuses on integrated pest management (IPM) and reduced-risk approaches. Increasingly important as clients — especially commercial and institutional — demand environmentally responsible pest management.

### International Licensing

If you are starting outside the United States:
- **United Kingdom:** Pest control operators typically obtain the RSPH Level 2 Award in Pest Management or the BPCA Certificate in Pest Management. No formal licensing is required by law, but most clients and commercial contracts require these qualifications.
- **Canada:** Provincial licensing through the ministry of environment. Requirements vary by province.
- **Australia:** Pest management licensing through state and territory authorities. Typically requires completing a Certificate III in Pest Management.
- **Ireland and EU:** Regulations vary by country. Professional use of biocides requires training certificates under the Biocidal Products Regulation.

> **Do Not Skip This**
Operating without proper licensing is not just a fine — it is a criminal offense in many jurisdictions. Penalties range from $1,000 to $25,000 per violation, and you can face personal liability for any harm caused by unlicensed pesticide applications. Your insurance will not cover you either. Get licensed before you treat a single property.

## Equipment Needed for Pest Control

Your equipment needs depend on which services you offer, but here is a comprehensive breakdown for a general pest control and rodent control startup.

### Spray Equipment ($2,000-$6,000)

- **B&G Sprayer (1-gallon compressed air sprayer):** $150 to $250. The industry standard for interior crack-and-crevice treatments. Every pest control technician owns at least one. Buy two — one for insecticides, one for a different product to avoid cross-contamination.
- **Backpack sprayer (4-gallon):** $150 to $400. Used for exterior perimeter treatments, lawn and yard applications. Battery-powered models ($300-$400) save your arm from pumping.
- **Power sprayer (truck-mounted or skid-mounted, 50-100 gallon):** $1,500 to $5,000. Essential for termite treatments, large exterior jobs, and mosquito control. Not needed immediately but should be on your year-one plan if you intend to do termite or mosquito work.

### Baiting and Trapping Equipment ($500-$1,500)

- **Rodent bait stations (exterior, tamper-resistant):** $8 to $20 each. Start with 50 to 100 units.
- **Interior rodent traps (snap traps and glue boards):** $200 to $300 initial stock.
- **Insect bait stations (gel bait applicators, granular bait dispensers):** $100 to $200.
- **Insect monitors and glue traps:** $100 to $200 for initial stock. Essential for monitoring and inspection.

### Application Tools ($500-$1,000)

- **Dust applicator (hand bellows or battery-powered duster):** $30 to $150. Used for applying insecticidal dust into wall voids, attics, and crevices.
- **Aerosol delivery system with injection tips:** $50 to $100. For treating cracks, crevices, and voids.
- **Foam applicator:** $100 to $200. For treating wall voids and hard-to-reach areas with expanding foam insecticides.
- **Granular spreader:** $30 to $80. For exterior granular bait applications.

### Personal Protective Equipment ($500-$1,000)

PPE is mandatory, not optional. OSHA and EPA regulations require specific PPE based on the products you use. Read every product label — it specifies the required PPE.

- **Respirators (half-face with organic vapor/P100 cartridges):** $30 to $50 each, plus $15 to $25 per set of replacement cartridges. Fit testing is required.
- **Chemical-resistant gloves (nitrile, neoprene):** $50 to $100 for a year's supply.
- **Safety glasses or goggles:** $10 to $30.
- **Protective coveralls (Tyvek or reusable):** $100 to $200 per year.
- **Chemical-resistant boots:** $60 to $120.

### Inspection Tools ($300-$600)

- **Flashlight (high-quality, 500+ lumens):** $30 to $80.
- **Telescoping inspection mirror:** $15 to $30.
- **Moisture meter:** $30 to $100. Essential for termite and wood-destroying organism inspections.
- **Borescope/inspection camera:** $100 to $300. Lets you see inside wall voids and tight spaces.
- **UV flashlight:** $15 to $40. Used for detecting rodent urine trails, scorpions, and certain insects.
- **Magnifying loupe or hand lens:** $10 to $20. For pest identification.

### Vehicle ($5,000-$25,000)

Your vehicle is your mobile office, warehouse, and billboard. A used cargo van or pickup truck with a service body or cap is ideal.

Requirements:
- Secure, lockable storage for chemicals (many states require this)
- Ventilated chemical storage compartment
- Space for equipment and supplies
- Professional appearance (vehicle wrap or magnetic signs)
- Reliable transportation — breakdowns mean missed appointments and lost clients

A used cargo van (Ford Transit, Chevy Express, Ram ProMaster) in good condition runs $12,000 to $25,000. A pickup truck with a service cap or utility body can work for $8,000 to $18,000.

Vehicle wrap or magnetic signs: $500 to $3,000. Magnetic signs are cheaper ($150-$300) and let you use the vehicle personally. A full wrap ($2,000-$3,000) looks more professional and serves as a mobile billboard.

### Chemical and Product Inventory ($500-$1,500 initial)

Your initial product inventory depends on your service offerings. General pest control basics include:

- Liquid residual insecticides (synthetic pyrethroids like Demand CS, Suspend SC, or Talstar P)
- Gel baits for cockroaches and ants (Vendetta, Advion, Alpine)
- Granular baits for exterior use (Niban, InTice)
- Insecticidal dust (Delta Dust, CimeXa, or diatomaceous earth)
- Rodenticide baits (for use in tamper-resistant stations only)
- Insect growth regulators (Gentrol, NyGuard)
- Non-repellent insecticides for ant and termite treatments (Termidor, Phantom, Alpine WSG)

Products are purchased through professional pest control distributors (Univar Solutions, Target Specialty Products, Veseris) — not consumer retailers. You will need your applicator license to open an account and purchase restricted-use products.

## Insurance Requirements

Pest control carries unique insurance requirements because you are applying chemicals to people's homes and businesses. Standard general liability is not enough.

### General Liability Insurance

Covers bodily injury and property damage claims from your operations. If a client slips on a treated surface, if your treatment damages landscaping, or if someone has an allergic reaction.

**Minimum coverage:** $1 million per occurrence, $2 million aggregate. Many commercial clients require higher limits.

**Cost:** $800 to $2,000 per year for a solo operator. Increases with revenue and employee count.

### Pollution Liability Insurance

This is the critical coverage most general contractors do not need but pest control operators absolutely do. Standard general liability policies typically exclude pollution-related claims. Pollution liability covers:

- Chemical spills or leaks from your vehicle or equipment
- Off-target pesticide drift
- Contamination of water sources, soil, or indoor air
- Health claims from pesticide exposure
- Cleanup and remediation costs

**Cost:** $1,000 to $3,000 per year. Worth every penny — a single chemical contamination claim can easily exceed $100,000.

### Commercial Auto Insurance

Your service vehicle carries chemicals, and you are driving to client sites all day. Personal auto insurance will not cover you. Commercial auto insurance covers accidents, chemical spills from the vehicle, and liability from vehicle operations.

**Cost:** $1,500 to $3,500 per year depending on vehicle type, driving history, and coverage limits.

### Workers' Compensation

Required in almost every state once you have employees. Pest control carries moderate workers' comp rates because of chemical exposure risks and physical labor.

**Cost:** Rates vary by state, typically $3 to $8 per $100 of payroll for pest control operators.

### Surety Bonds

Many states require pest control operators to carry a surety bond, typically $5,000 to $15,000. This protects consumers if you fail to complete work or cause damage. Cost is a percentage of the bond amount — usually 1% to 10% depending on your credit score, so a $10,000 bond costs $100 to $1,000 per year.

## Treatment Methods and Approaches

Understanding treatment methods is fundamental to delivering effective service and explaining your value to clients.

### Integrated Pest Management (IPM)

IPM is the industry standard approach and is required for many commercial and institutional accounts. It prioritizes long-term prevention over reactive treatment.

The IPM hierarchy:
1. **Inspection and identification** — correctly identify the pest before treating
2. **Sanitation and habitat modification** — eliminate food, water, and harborage
3. **Physical and mechanical controls** — exclusion, trapping, barriers
4. **Biological controls** — natural predators, parasites (more common in agriculture)
5. **Chemical controls** — targeted pesticide applications as a last resort or supplementary measure

In practice, most residential pest control combines elements of all these. You seal entry points, set monitors, apply targeted baits and residual products, and educate clients on sanitation. The days of spraying baseboards with broad-spectrum insecticides are over — IPM is more effective, safer, and more profitable because it emphasizes recurring monitoring and prevention.

### Chemical Application Methods

- **Crack and crevice treatment:** Precise application of liquid, aerosol, or dust formulations into cracks, crevices, voids, and harborage areas. Minimizes exposure and targets pests where they live.
- **Spot treatment:** Application to specific, limited areas where pests are active or likely to travel.
- **Perimeter treatment:** Liquid barrier application around the exterior foundation, typically 2-3 feet up the wall and 2-3 feet out from the foundation. Creates a treated zone that pests must cross to enter the structure.
- **Void treatment:** Injection of dust, aerosol, or foam into wall voids, attics, and other enclosed spaces through small drilled holes or existing openings.
- **Baiting:** Placement of insecticidal or rodenticidal baits in strategic locations. Gel baits for cockroaches and ants, granular baits for exterior insects, bait stations for rodents.
- **Fumigation:** Tenting and gas treatment of entire structures. Requires specialized licensing, equipment, and is typically done by larger companies. Not recommended as a startup service.

### Non-Chemical Methods

- **Exclusion:** Sealing gaps, cracks, and openings that allow pest entry. Copper mesh, steel wool, caulk, expanding foam, door sweeps, and vent screens. High margin work — materials cost $5 to $30, labor drives the price.
- **Trapping:** Snap traps, live traps, glue monitors. Primary method for indoor rodent control and monitoring programs.
- **Heat treatment:** Raising room temperature to lethal levels for bed bugs and other pests. Requires specialized equipment.
- **Moisture control:** Dehumidification and drainage recommendations to reduce conditions favorable to wood-destroying organisms, silverfish, and other moisture-loving pests.

## Pricing Your Pest Control Services

Pricing is where many new pest control companies go wrong — either too low (racing to the bottom) or inconsistently structured (making it hard to scale). Here is how to build a pricing model that supports a profitable business.

### Residential Pricing Models

**Per-Service Pricing (One-Time Treatments):**
- General pest control (initial service): $175 to $350
- General pest control (follow-up/routine): $100 to $175
- Rodent control (initial + exclusion): $250 to $600
- Rodent control (trapping follow-up): $75 to $150
- Bed bug treatment (chemical, per room): $300 to $500
- Bed bug treatment (heat, whole home): $1,500 to $4,000
- Termite treatment (liquid barrier): $1,500 to $3,000
- Termite baiting system installation: $1,500 to $2,500
- Mosquito treatment (per visit): $75 to $150
- Wasp/hornet nest removal: $100 to $350

**Subscription/Recurring Plans (Monthly or Quarterly):**
This is where the real money is. Recurring plans provide predictable revenue and high customer lifetime value.

- Monthly general pest control: $40 to $60 per month ($480-$720/year)
- Quarterly general pest control: $100 to $175 per quarter ($400-$700/year)
- Quarterly pest + annual termite inspection: $150 to $225 per quarter ($600-$900/year)
- Premium plan (pest + termite + mosquito seasonal): $75 to $125 per month ($900-$1,500/year)

**Average customer lifetime:** 3 to 5 years on recurring plans. A quarterly customer at $125 per quarter has a lifetime value of $1,500 to $2,500. Acquiring that customer at a cost of $50 to $200 makes the math excellent.

### Commercial Pricing

Commercial pest control is priced based on facility size, risk level, pest pressure, service frequency, and industry requirements.

- **Restaurants:** $125 to $300 per month depending on size and frequency. High service intensity due to health code requirements.
- **Offices:** $75 to $200 per month. Lower intensity but consistent.
- **Retail:** $100 to $250 per month depending on type (food retail requires more).
- **Healthcare:** $200 to $500 per month. Stringent documentation and IPM requirements.
- **Warehouses and industrial:** $150 to $500 per month depending on size and pest risk.
- **Food processing and manufacturing:** $300 to $1,000+ per month. Highest requirements, audit compliance, detailed documentation.
- **Property management (multi-family):** $15 to $30 per unit per month on master contracts.

Commercial contracts are typically annual with automatic renewal. They provide the stable revenue base that makes your business valuable and sellable.

### How to Calculate Your Pricing

Start with your costs:

1. **Direct costs per service stop:** Chemical and material costs ($5-$25), vehicle costs (fuel, maintenance allocated per stop), and your labor time
2. **Overhead allocation:** Insurance, licensing, software, marketing, office expenses, divided by total monthly service stops
3. **Target margin:** 55% to 70% gross margin on residential, 45% to 60% on commercial
4. **Market check:** Compare to competitors in your area (get quotes as a mystery shopper)

**Example residential quarterly service pricing:**
- Direct costs per stop: $15 (chemicals, materials)
- Labor cost (45 minutes including drive time): $25
- Overhead allocation per stop: $20
- Total cost: $60
- At 65% gross margin: $60 / (1 - 0.65) = $171
- Round to $175 per quarterly service

> **Pro Tip**
Push for recurring plans, not one-time services. A one-time general pest treatment at $225 is worth $225. That same customer on a quarterly plan at $150 per quarter is worth $600 per year and $2,400 over four years. Structure your pricing to make recurring plans the obvious choice — make one-time pricing notably higher than the annualized recurring rate.

## Marketing Your Pest Control Business

Pest control marketing is different from other service businesses because of the trust factor. You are asking people to let you into their home and apply chemicals around their family and pets. Every marketing touchpoint must build trust and demonstrate competence.

### Google Business Profile (Priority One)

Your Google Business Profile is the single most important marketing asset. When someone finds a cockroach in their kitchen at 10 PM, they search "pest control near me" on their phone. The companies in the Google Maps 3-pack get the calls.

Set up and optimize immediately:
- Complete every field — services, hours, service area, business description
- Add photos of your truck, equipment, team, and completed work (before/after when possible)
- Post regularly (weekly updates, tips, seasonal pest alerts)
- Respond to every review, positive or negative
- Target reviews aggressively — 50+ reviews significantly improve your ranking

### Website and SEO

Your website needs to convert visitors into leads. Essential pages:

- **Home page:** Clear value proposition, service list, phone number, and call-to-action
- **Service pages:** Individual pages for each service (general pest, rodents, termites, etc.) with specific information about the pests, your treatment approach, and pricing starting points
- **Service area pages:** If you serve multiple cities or counties, create a page for each
- **About page:** Your story, credentials, licensing information, team photos
- **Contact/quote request page:** Simple form, phone number, chat widget

SEO for pest control focuses on local search terms: "pest control [city]," "exterminator near [neighborhood]," "termite treatment [county]." Each service page and location page should target specific keyword combinations.

### Google Ads (Pay-Per-Click)

Google Ads delivers the fastest results in pest control marketing. People searching "emergency pest control" or "bed bug exterminator" have immediate intent — they need help now.

- **Google Local Service Ads (LSA):** Pay per lead, not per click. You appear at the very top of search results with a "Google Guaranteed" badge. LSA leads cost $20 to $60 each, and close rates are high because the customer has already seen your reviews and verified status.
- **Standard Search Ads:** Target high-intent keywords. Cost per click ranges from $5 to $30 depending on your market and the pest type. Bed bug and termite clicks are the most expensive; general pest control is cheaper.

Start with $500 to $1,500 per month in ad spend. Track cost per lead and cost per acquisition religiously. A well-managed campaign should deliver leads at $25 to $75 each and new customers at $75 to $200 each.

### Referral Programs

Pest control is inherently referral-friendly. When your neighbor sees the pest control truck at your house, they ask about it. Formalize this with a structured referral program:

- Offer $25 to $50 credit (or cash) for every referral that becomes a customer
- Give the referred customer a discount on their first service ($25 off)
- Remind clients about the referral program on every service visit and in follow-up communications
- Track referral sources meticulously

### Partnerships and Networking

Build relationships with complementary businesses:
- **Real estate agents:** Home inspections often reveal pest issues, and agents need reliable referral partners for their clients
- **Property managers:** Ongoing contracts for multi-family and commercial properties
- **Home inspectors:** Referral relationships for termite inspections (some states require separate WDO inspectors)
- **Contractors and builders:** New construction pre-treatments and renovation pest control
- **HVAC companies, plumbers, electricians:** These trades work inside homes and see pest problems — cross-referral relationships are mutually beneficial

### Seasonal Marketing Strategies

Pest control demand is seasonal in most markets. Align your marketing with pest activity:

- **Spring (March-May):** Ant season, termite swarm season, wasps becoming active. Push termite inspections, spring pest prevention plans.
- **Summer (June-August):** Peak pest season. Mosquito services, wasp removal, general pest control. Highest demand — you should be fully booked.
- **Fall (September-November):** Overwintering pests (stink bugs, lady beetles, mice) seek shelter. Exclusion services, rodent prevention, fall perimeter treatments.
- **Winter (December-February):** Rodent peak season. Indoor pest issues (cockroaches, spiders). Use downtime for commercial account prospecting and equipment maintenance.

## Safety Protocols

Safety is non-negotiable in pest control. You are working with regulated chemicals, entering confined spaces, climbing on roofs, and dealing with stinging insects and potentially diseased wildlife.

### Chemical Safety

- **Read every product label.** The label is the law. It specifies application rates, target pests, allowed surfaces, required PPE, re-entry intervals, and environmental precautions.
- **Maintain Safety Data Sheets (SDS)** for every product you use. Keep copies in your vehicle, at your office, and digitally. You are legally required to make SDS available to employees, clients (on request), and emergency responders.
- **Wear required PPE** for every application. No exceptions.
- **Never mix products** unless specifically instructed by the label.
- **Secure all chemicals** in locked compartments in your vehicle and in locked storage at your facility.
- **Properly dispose of empty containers and rinseate** according to label instructions and local regulations.
- **Document everything:** What product was applied, where, how much, what concentration, wind speed and direction (for exterior applications), and target pest.

### Physical Safety

- **Ladder safety:** Use proper equipment for attic and roof access. Falls are the leading cause of injury in pest control.
- **Confined space awareness:** Crawl spaces and attics present heat stress, respiratory hazards, and physical hazards.
- **Stinging insect precautions:** Carry an emergency epinephrine kit and know how to use it. Wasp and hornet removal is inherently dangerous.
- **Animal encounters:** Rodent work can expose you to hantavirus, leptospirosis, and other diseases. Use PPE and proper sanitation.
- **Vehicle safety:** Secure all chemical containers during transport. Ensure ventilation in storage compartments.

### Liability Protection Practices

- **Get written authorization** before every treatment. This includes client consent, a description of the treatment plan, products to be used, and any preparation requirements.
- **Communicate clearly** about pet and child safety precautions, re-entry times, and ventilation requirements.
- **Document conditions** before treatment. Note existing damage, stains, pet areas, and anything that could become a liability dispute.
- **Follow up** after every treatment. A quick call or message asking if the client has questions reduces complaints and builds trust.

## Scaling from Solo to Team

The transition from solo operator to managing technicians is the most challenging phase in growing a pest control company. Here is how to approach it methodically.

### Phase 1: Solo Operator (Months 1-6)

Focus on building your client base and perfecting your systems.

- Target: 80-120 recurring accounts
- Revenue target: $6,000 to $12,000 per month
- You do everything — sales, service, admin, marketing
- Use this time to build standard operating procedures for every service type
- Create checklists and documentation templates
- Build your reputation and collect reviews

### Phase 2: First Hire (Months 6-12)

Hire when you are consistently turning away work or cannot add more appointments to your schedule.

- Hire a technician, not a salesperson. You need someone who can perform services while you split time between field work and sales/admin.
- Look for candidates with: reliability (above all else), pest control experience or aptitude, clean driving record, ability to pass background check, comfort working independently
- Start them with a training ride-along period (2-4 weeks minimum) where they accompany you on all jobs
- Pay structure: $16 to $22 per hour starting, or $35,000 to $50,000 salary depending on experience and market
- They handle service while you handle sales, estimates, and business development

### Phase 3: Growth Mode (Year 2)

- Add technicians as revenue justifies (each technician should generate $8,000-$15,000 per month in revenue)
- Hire an office coordinator or invest in [scheduling software](/product/scheduling) to manage dispatch
- Transition yourself out of daily service work into full-time management and sales
- Implement performance metrics: services per day, revenue per route day, customer retention rate, callback rate
- Formalize training programs for new hires

### Phase 4: Manager-Run Operation (Year 3+)

- Promote or hire a service manager to oversee daily operations
- Add specialized services (termite, bed bug, wildlife) as team capabilities grow
- Pursue commercial accounts that require larger capacity
- Consider multiple service vehicles and territories
- Evaluate franchise or additional location opportunities

### Key Metrics to Track

- **Revenue per route day:** Total daily revenue per technician. Target $800-$1,500.
- **Stops per day:** Number of service appointments completed. Target 8-14 depending on service type.
- **Customer retention rate:** Percentage of recurring customers who renew. Target 80%+ annually.
- **Callback rate:** Percentage of services requiring free re-treatment. Target below 5%.
- **Cost per acquisition:** Marketing spend divided by new customers acquired. Target $75-$200.
- **Average revenue per customer:** Annual revenue per account. Target $500+ for residential.

- **80-120** — recurring accounts target for solo phase
- **80%+** — annual customer retention rate target
- **$800-$1,500** — revenue per route day target

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### Skipping Licensing

This is the most dangerous mistake. Operating without a license exposes you to fines up to $25,000 per violation, criminal charges, and personal liability for any harm caused. No shortcut is worth this risk.

### Underbidding to Win Accounts

New pest control operators often price below the market to win business. This attracts price-sensitive clients who leave the moment someone cheaper comes along, and it leaves you without enough margin to deliver quality service or invest in growth. Price for value, not for volume.

### Neglecting Recurring Revenue

One-time pest treatments are low-margin, high-churn revenue. The companies that build lasting value are built on recurring service plans. Structure your entire business model around getting customers onto quarterly or monthly plans.

### Poor Documentation

Every treatment should be documented — products used, quantities, application locations, target pests, conditions observed. This protects you legally, supports your pest management recommendations, and is required for most commercial accounts and certifications.

### Not Getting Pollution Liability Insurance

Standard general liability does not cover chemical contamination claims. One spill, one off-target application, one contamination claim without pollution liability coverage could bankrupt you. This is non-negotiable for pest control.

### Trying to Do Everything Immediately

Start with general pest control and rodent control. Master those services, build your client base, and add specialty services as you gain experience and capital. Trying to offer termite, bed bug, wildlife, mosquito, and fumigation services from day one spreads you too thin and increases the risk of costly mistakes.
