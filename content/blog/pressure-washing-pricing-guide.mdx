# How to Price Pressure Washing Jobs: Rates, Formulas & Calculator

Pressure washing pricing is more nuanced than most operators realise. The difference between a profitable job and a money-losing one often comes down to how accurately you account for variables like surface type, chemical requirements, water access, and setup time. Underprice consistently, and you wear out your equipment faster than you can replace it. Overprice, and you lose bids to competitors who understand their numbers better.

This guide gives you the frameworks, formulas, and real-world rates you need to price [pressure washing](/pressure-washing) jobs with confidence in 2026 — whether you are quoting a residential driveway or a commercial fleet wash.

## Pressure Washing Pricing Models

Like general cleaning services, pressure washing can be priced using three main models. The right model depends on the job type, your experience level, and your client's expectations.

### Per Square Foot Pricing

This is the most common and most transparent model for pressure washing. You measure or estimate the surface area, multiply by your rate per square foot, and present a clear, defensible quote.

**When to use it:** Driveways, patios, decks, siding, parking lots, and any large flat or semi-flat surface where square footage is easy to estimate. This model works best when the surface condition is relatively uniform.

**How to calculate:** Measure the length and width of the surface area in feet. Multiply to get total square footage. Multiply by your per-square-foot rate for that surface type.

**Example:** A 600-square-foot concrete driveway at $0.15 per square foot = $90. But if the driveway is heavily stained with oil and requires pre-treatment, your rate might be $0.25 per square foot = $150.

**Pros:** Clients understand it. Easy to justify your quote. Scales logically with job size. Allows you to quote remotely if the client provides dimensions.

**Cons:** Does not account for difficulty, access issues, or surface condition. Requires adjustment factors for different scenarios.

### Hourly Pricing

With hourly pricing, you charge a flat rate per hour of work including setup and breakdown time.

**When to use it:** Jobs with unpredictable scope, such as heavily stained surfaces, mould remediation, graffiti removal, or properties where you cannot assess the condition until you arrive. Also useful when you are starting out and have not yet developed accurate per-square-foot estimating skills.

**Typical 2026 rates:** $75 to $150 per hour for a single operator with a professional-grade machine. Higher rates ($100 to $200+) are justified for hot water machines, specialist surface restoration, or commercial work requiring insurance certifications.

**Pros:** Protects you on difficult jobs. Simple to calculate. No risk of underquoting.

**Cons:** Clients dislike open-ended billing. Creates an incentive (perceived or real) to work slowly. Harder to sell because the client does not know the final cost upfront.

### Flat-Rate Pricing

Flat-rate pricing assigns a fixed price to common job types regardless of the exact square footage. You build a menu of standard services with set prices.

**When to use it:** Routine residential jobs where you have enough experience to estimate time accurately. Standard driveway washes, house washes, deck cleans, and fence washes are all good candidates for flat-rate pricing.

**Pros:** Fastest quoting method. Clients love the certainty. Rewards your efficiency — the faster you complete the job, the higher your effective hourly rate. Simplifies your marketing because you can advertise starting prices.

**Cons:** Requires experience to set rates accurately. You absorb the risk on jobs that take longer than expected. Need clear scope definitions to prevent scope creep.

**Example menu:**
- Standard driveway (up to 600 sq ft): $120-$180
- Double driveway (up to 1,200 sq ft): $200-$320
- Single-storey house wash: $250-$450
- Two-storey house wash: $400-$700
- Standard deck (up to 300 sq ft): $150-$250

## Average Rates by Job Type (2026)

These rates represent typical pricing in metropolitan and suburban markets across Australia, the US, and the UK. Rural areas may be lower; high-cost-of-living cities may be significantly higher.

### Driveways and Paths

- **Concrete driveway:** $0.10-$0.25 per sq ft, or $100-$300 flat rate depending on size
- **Paver driveway:** $0.15-$0.30 per sq ft (pavers require lower pressure and more care)
- **Footpaths and walkways:** $0.12-$0.20 per sq ft, minimum charge of $80-$120

Driveways are the most common residential pressure washing job. They are also the best entry point for upselling additional services — once you are on-site with your equipment, offering to wash the paths, patio, or house exterior for an additional fee is a natural conversation.

### Decks and Outdoor Living Areas

- **Timber deck:** $0.20-$0.40 per sq ft (requires careful pressure settings to avoid wood damage)
- **Composite deck:** $0.15-$0.30 per sq ft
- **Concrete patio:** $0.10-$0.25 per sq ft
- **Pool surrounds:** $0.15-$0.30 per sq ft (non-slip surfaces require specialised tips)

Deck washing often leads to sealing work, which is a high-margin upsell. Always quote the seal as a separate line item so the client can see the added value.

### House Siding and Exteriors

- **Vinyl or aluminium siding:** $0.15-$0.35 per sq ft
- **Brick exterior:** $0.15-$0.40 per sq ft
- **Stucco/render:** $0.20-$0.45 per sq ft (requires soft washing technique)
- **Whole house wash (single storey):** $250-$500 flat rate
- **Whole house wash (two storey):** $400-$800 flat rate

House washing almost always requires soft washing — a low-pressure application of cleaning solution followed by a gentle rinse. Using high pressure on siding, stucco, or painted surfaces causes damage. Price soft wash work slightly higher than standard pressure washing to account for the chemical costs and the additional skill required.

### Commercial and Industrial

- **Parking lots:** $0.05-$0.15 per sq ft (large area, lower per-sq-ft rate, but high total job value)
- **Building exteriors:** $0.10-$0.30 per sq ft depending on height and access requirements
- **Drive-throughs:** $150-$400 per service
- **Dumpster pads:** $75-$200 per pad
- **Loading docks:** $200-$500 depending on size

Commercial work typically involves recurring contracts — monthly, quarterly, or bi-annual — which provides predictable revenue. Quote a per-service rate with a discount for multi-service contracts. A quarterly parking lot wash might be $800 per visit, but a twelve-month contract with quarterly service could be $700 per visit.

### Fleet and Vehicle Washing

- **Single truck/van:** $30-$75 per vehicle
- **Semi-trailer:** $50-$120 per unit
- **Fleet contract (10+ vehicles):** $20-$50 per vehicle with volume discount
- **Heavy equipment:** $100-$300+ depending on size and condition

Fleet washing is a volume business. The margins per vehicle are lower, but the predictable recurring revenue and minimal quoting overhead make it attractive. Use [custom forms](/product/custom-forms) to track vehicle condition before and after each wash for quality documentation.

## How to Factor In Your True Costs

Your pricing must cover more than just your time on the trigger. Here is every cost category you need to account for.

### Water Usage

Most residential pressure washers use 2 to 4 gallons per minute (GPM). A typical driveway clean takes 30 to 60 minutes, consuming 60 to 240 gallons of water. If you are paying for water through a metered connection, calculate your cost per gallon and factor it into your per-job cost. In most areas, water cost per job is $1 to $5 — small but not negligible over hundreds of jobs.

If you are using the client's water supply, this cost is zero to you, but note that some commercial clients may require you to supply your own water via a tank system, which adds significant cost and setup time.

### Chemical Costs

Soft washing and surface treatment require cleaning chemicals: sodium hypochlorite, surfactants, degreasers, rust removers, or specialised solutions for specific stain types. Track your chemical cost per job by measuring how much solution you use on each job type.

Typical chemical costs:
- **House soft wash:** $10-$30 per job (sodium hypochlorite + surfactant)
- **Concrete degreasing:** $5-$15 per driveway
- **Deck preparation (brightener/stripper):** $15-$40 per deck
- **Rust removal:** $10-$25 per application area

Buy chemicals in bulk to reduce per-job costs. A 20-litre container of sodium hypochlorite costs far less per litre than individual bottles from a hardware store.

### Equipment Wear and Depreciation

Pressure washing equipment takes a beating. High-quality machines last 1,000 to 3,000 hours of use before requiring major service or replacement. Calculate your depreciation cost per hour:

**Equipment depreciation per hour = Purchase price / Expected lifetime hours**

A $3,000 machine with an expected life of 2,000 hours costs $1.50 per operating hour in depreciation. Add hoses ($200-$500, replaced annually), nozzles ($5-$20 each, replaced every few months), surface cleaners ($150-$400, annual replacement), and unloader valves ($50-$150, as needed).

A reasonable total equipment cost is $3 to $8 per operating hour. Factor this into every quote.

### Travel Time and Vehicle Costs

Pressure washing involves hauling heavy equipment, which means higher fuel consumption than a standard vehicle. Calculate your cost per mile or kilometre including fuel, insurance, registration, maintenance, trailer registration (if applicable), and vehicle depreciation.

Include setup and breakdown time in your pricing — loading the trailer, driving to the site, unloading equipment, connecting to water, and reversing the process after the job. This typically adds 30 to 60 minutes to each job that you are not directly billing for unless your pricing accounts for it.

### Insurance

Pressure washing requires higher insurance coverage than general cleaning due to the risk of property damage. A misplaced high-pressure stream can etch glass, strip paint, damage timber, or crack rendering. Your public liability insurance for pressure washing work typically costs $1,000 to $3,000 per year. Spread this cost across your expected annual job count.

## Seasonal Pricing Strategies

Pressure washing demand is highly seasonal in most markets. Understanding the seasonal cycle helps you optimise pricing and manage cash flow.

**Spring (peak season):** Demand surges as homeowners prepare for outdoor entertaining. This is when you should charge your full rates — no discounts needed. Focus on house washes, deck cleans, and patio preparation. Book out weeks in advance and consider hiring seasonal help.

**Summer:** Steady demand, particularly for commercial work (restaurants, retail, event venues). Residential demand remains strong for pool areas, outdoor kitchens, and pre-event cleaning. Maintain full pricing.

**Autumn/Fall:** Demand begins to taper. Offer "winterise your outdoor spaces" packages that combine pressure washing with sealing services. This is a good time to lock in commercial contracts for the coming year.

**Winter (low season):** Residential demand drops significantly in cold climates. Focus on commercial work (parking lots, drive-throughs, industrial facilities) that runs year-round. Consider offering a modest discount (10-15%) to fill your schedule, or use the downtime for equipment maintenance, marketing, and business development.

In warm-climate markets (Florida, Queensland, Southern California), the seasonal swing is less pronounced, but you may see dips during the hottest months when outdoor work is physically demanding and clients defer projects.

## How to Quote Jobs Remotely Using Photos

Not every job requires an on-site visit before quoting. For standard residential work, you can often provide an accurate quote using photos and a few key measurements from the client.

### What to Request

Ask the client to send:
1. **Overview photos** of each surface to be cleaned (wide angle showing the full area)
2. **Close-up photos** of any heavily stained or damaged areas
3. **Approximate dimensions** — most homeowners know their driveway length and width, or you can estimate using Google Maps satellite view
4. **Photos of water access points** (outdoor tap location, hose bib condition)
5. **Any access restrictions** (locked gates, steep terrain, overhead power lines near the wash area)

### How to Estimate from Photos

With experience, you can assess surface condition from photos with reasonable accuracy. Look for:
- **Colour variance** — heavy biological growth (mould, algae, lichen) appears as dark staining and adds time
- **Oil stains** — dark spots on driveways indicate degreasing will be needed
- **Surface texture** — exposed aggregate concrete takes longer than smooth-trowelled concrete
- **Obstructions** — furniture, potted plants, vehicles, and garden beds that need to be worked around

Add a 15-20% buffer to your remote quote and include a clause allowing adjustment after on-site assessment. Use your [pricing calculator](/tools/pricing-calculator) to run the numbers based on square footage and surface type, then apply your condition multiplier.

Create a standardised quoting form using [custom forms](/product/custom-forms) that clients fill out with their property details and upload photos. This streamlines the quoting process and ensures you collect consistent information from every prospect.

## Upselling Strategies

The most profitable pressure washing businesses do not just wash surfaces — they offer complementary services that increase the value of every job.

### Sealing and Coating

After washing concrete, pavers, or timber, offer a protective sealant application. Sealing extends the clean appearance by twelve to twenty-four months and protects against future staining. Price sealing at $0.20 to $0.50 per square foot on top of the washing price. Your material cost is $0.05 to $0.15 per square foot, making this a high-margin add-on.

**How to sell it:** "Your driveway looks great now. A sealant application will keep it looking this way for the next eighteen months and protect it from oil stains and tyre marks. It adds $X to today's job."

### Soft Washing Add-Ons

If you are on-site washing a driveway, offer to soft wash the house exterior, fences, or retaining walls. The marginal cost of adding work while your equipment is already set up is much lower than mobilising for a separate visit.

**How to sell it:** "While I'm here with everything set up, I can wash your fence line too. It would be $X as an add-on, compared to $Y if you booked it as a separate visit."

### Gutter Cleaning

Gutter cleaning pairs naturally with house washing. Offer it as a package deal. Most homeowners who care enough about their exterior to book a pressure wash also need their gutters cleaned. Price gutter cleaning at $1.50 to $4.00 per linear metre, or offer a flat rate based on home size.

### Recurring Maintenance Plans

Convert one-off clients into recurring revenue by offering quarterly or bi-annual maintenance plans at a discounted rate. A driveway that costs $180 as a one-off could be offered at $150 per visit on a bi-annual plan — the client saves money, and you lock in predictable revenue.

## Common Pricing Mistakes in Pressure Washing

### Underestimating Setup and Breakdown Time

Loading your trailer, driving to the site, unloading equipment, connecting hoses, and reversing the process takes thirty to sixty minutes per job. If you are only pricing for trigger time, you are working for free during setup. Include a minimum job charge ($100-$150) that covers your fixed time cost regardless of the surface area.

### Ignoring Surface Condition

A lightly soiled driveway and a driveway covered in years of mould, oil stains, and tyre marks are completely different jobs, even if they are the same size. Always assess condition — either in person or from photos — and adjust your per-square-foot rate accordingly. A condition multiplier of 1.0x (light soiling) to 2.0x (heavy soiling with pre-treatment needed) keeps your pricing fair and profitable.

### Not Having a Minimum Job Charge

Small jobs — a single set of stairs, a 100-square-foot patio, a single dumpster pad — can be money losers if you price them purely on square footage. Set a minimum charge that covers your mobilisation cost: fuel, travel time, setup, and a reasonable margin. Most operators set their minimum at $100 to $200. Any job that calculates below the minimum gets quoted at the minimum.

### Pricing Commercial Work Like Residential

Commercial clients expect professional documentation: written quotes, certificates of insurance, safety compliance, and detailed invoicing. They also pay slower — thirty-day payment terms are standard. Price commercial work to cover this additional admin overhead and the cash flow impact of delayed payment. Your commercial rates should be 10-20% higher than residential rates for equivalent work, plus a markup for documentation and compliance requirements.

### Failing to Account for Water Restrictions

In drought-prone areas, water restrictions can affect your ability to complete jobs or require you to use water reclamation systems. If you operate in an area with seasonal water restrictions, factor the cost of water tanks, reclamation equipment, or restricted operating hours into your seasonal pricing.

### Not Tracking Job Profitability

After completing a job, compare your actual time and materials against your quote. Were you profitable? Did the job take longer than estimated? Did you use more chemicals than expected? Track this data for every job type and use it to refine your pricing over time. Without data, you are guessing — and guessing consistently favours the client, not you.

## Building Your Pricing System

The most successful pressure washing operators do not quote from memory or gut feeling. They build a pricing system based on data from completed jobs, update it regularly, and use it consistently.

Your pricing system should include:
1. **Base rates per square foot** for each surface type (concrete, pavers, timber, siding, brick)
2. **Condition multipliers** (1.0x to 2.0x) based on soiling level
3. **A minimum job charge** that covers mobilisation
4. **Chemical cost estimates** by job type
5. **Add-on pricing** for sealing, soft washing, gutter cleaning, and other extras
6. **Seasonal adjustment factors** for peak and off-peak periods

Feed your historical job data into a [pricing calculator](/tools/pricing-calculator) to validate your rates against your actual costs and margins. Adjust quarterly based on the data. Over time, your estimates become highly accurate, your close rate improves because your pricing is competitive and fair, and your margins stay healthy because you are never unknowingly working below cost.

Pressure washing is a physically demanding business with significant equipment investment. Your pricing should reflect the skill, equipment, insurance, and professionalism you bring to every job — not just the time you spend holding a wand. Price with confidence, track your numbers, and let the data guide your decisions.
